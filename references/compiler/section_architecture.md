# Section Architecture

## Principle

Every section in a piece of writing has a job. If the section does not perform
its declared job, it either needs rewriting or removal. Sections are not
containers for loosely related paragraphs — they are structural units with
entry conditions, internal logic, and exit requirements.

---

## Section Jobs

### Define
Purpose: Establish what something is, what it covers, what it excludes.
Entry condition: Reader does not yet share the writer's definition.
Internal logic: Term → scope → boundary → implication.
Exit requirement: Reader can now use the term as the writer intends.
Fail state: Definition is circular, vague, or borrowed without attribution.

### Prove
Purpose: Establish that a claim is true.
Entry condition: Claim has been stated; reader is not yet convinced.
Internal logic: Claim → evidence → interpretation → evidence → interpretation → conclusion.
Exit requirement: A reasonable skeptic would accept the claim or know exactly where they disagree.
Fail state: Section only asserts. Evidence is absent, weak, or fabricated.

### Contrast
Purpose: Show how two things differ in ways that matter.
Entry condition: Reader might confuse A and B, or might not see why the difference matters.
Internal logic: A's characteristics → B's characteristics → point of divergence → stakes of the difference.
Exit requirement: Reader understands not just THAT they differ but WHY it matters.
Fail state: Contrast is cosmetic (A and B are essentially the same) or the stakes are unstated.

### Narrate
Purpose: Move through events in sequence.
Entry condition: Reader needs temporal or causal context.
Internal logic: Event → consequence → event → consequence, with sensory or contextual grounding.
Exit requirement: Reader has a mental timeline and understands causation.
Fail state: Events listed without causation. Or: narrative exists for color without advancing the argument.

### Warn
Purpose: Alert the reader to a risk, failure mode, or danger.
Entry condition: Reader may not be aware of the risk or may underestimate it.
Internal logic: Risk identification → mechanism → evidence of past occurrence → severity → mitigation (optional).
Exit requirement: Reader takes the risk seriously.
Fail state: Warning is vague ("be careful") or unsupported by evidence.

### Persuade
Purpose: Move the reader toward a position or action.
Entry condition: Reader is neutral, skeptical, or opposed.
Internal logic: Acknowledge current position → introduce tension → present evidence → address strongest counterargument → resolve.
Exit requirement: Reader's position has shifted or they understand why it should.
Fail state: Persuasion relies on emotion without evidence, or on authority without argument.

### Operationalize
Purpose: Convert insight into action.
Entry condition: Reader understands the concept and needs to implement it.
Internal logic: What to do → in what order → with what resources → what success looks like → what failure looks like.
Exit requirement: Reader can begin implementation.
Fail state: Instructions are too abstract to act on. Or: they assume capabilities the reader doesn't have.

### Close
Purpose: Resolve the argument and leave the reader with something lasting.
Entry condition: All evidence has been presented. All positions considered.
Internal logic: Synthesis → implication → challenge or image or earned question.
Exit requirement: Reader feels the piece is complete AND carries something forward.
Fail state: Summary. Repetition. Platitude. "Only time will tell."

## Section Scoring

For each section, answer:

1. What job did this section declare (or imply)?
2. Did it perform that job? (Yes / Partially / No)
3. If partially, what's missing?
4. Does the section overlap with another section's territory?
5. Is the section in the right position relative to the others?
6. Could the section be compressed without losing its function?

A section that scores "No" on question 2 must be rewritten or removed.

---

## Section Sequencing

Common effective sequences:

- **Argument**: Define → Prove → Contrast → Prove → Close
- **Strategy document**: Define → Warn → Operationalize → Prove → Close
- **Narrative**: Narrate → Define → Prove → Narrate → Close
- **Persuasion**: Narrate → Warn → Prove → Persuade → Operationalize → Close
- **Academic essay**: Define → Prove → Contrast → Prove → Prove → Close

The sequence should escalate. The reader should feel the argument building,
not meandering.

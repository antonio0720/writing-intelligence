## Part I: Core Laws

### Law 1: Every Character Has a Voice Fingerprint

If you cover the attribution tags and can't tell who's speaking, the dialogue
has failed. Distinction comes from:

- **Vocabulary tier**: A hedge fund manager and a mechanic don't use the same words
- **Sentence length**: Some people speak in fragments. Others in paragraphs. Most are somewhere specific.
- **Verbal habits**: Repeated phrases, filler words, pet expressions
- **What they DON'T say**: Evasion patterns, topics they avoid, emotions they suppress
- **Rhythm**: Staccato vs. flowing. Precise vs. rambling. Direct vs. circumlocutory.
- **Relationship modulation**: People speak differently to their mother, their boss, their enemy, and their child. A character who speaks the same way to everyone is a flat character.

### Law 2: Dialogue is Not Conversation

Real conversation is full of filler, repetition, and dead ends. Dialogue is
conversation with purpose — every exchange advances character, plot, or theme.
The craft is making it FEEL real while being compressed.

Humans use filler: um, uh, like, uh huh, you know. Put these in the mouths of
characters and the fictional illusion crumbles. Dialogue is shorn down to reveal
what people want from one another, reveal character, and dramatize power
struggles.

### Law 3: Subtext Over Text

People rarely say what they mean. They say something adjacent and let the
listener decode it. The gap between what's said and what's meant is where
dialogue gets its power.

"Are you coming to dinner?" means: "Are we still together?"
"I'm fine" means: "I am not fine and I need you to notice."
"That's an interesting approach" means: "That's wrong."
"He seems nice" means: "He is a tool of the highest order."

There are always wide gaps between what people say and what they think,
between what one understands and what one refuses to hear. These gaps
are subtext, and they are the most valuable territory in dialogue writing.

### Law 4: Conflict is the Engine

Every dialogue scene needs tension. Not necessarily argument — tension.
Unequal information. Competing desires. Hidden agendas. Power asymmetry.
Two people who want the same thing and agree about everything do not need
a scene.

**Logical conflict vs. manufactured conflict**: Logical conflict is baked into
character traits, competing desires, and asymmetric information. It feels
natural and inevitable. Manufactured conflict is injected artificially — characters
screaming at each other because "conflict is good." The word for manufactured
conflict is "contrived." Viewers and readers are too sophisticated for it.

### Law 5: Said is Invisible

The reader's brain skips "said." It processes "exclaimed," "retorted,"
"opined," "queried" as speed bumps. Use "said" and "asked" for 90%+
of attribution. Let the dialogue itself carry the emotion.

Never: "he said angrily." Instead: write angry words.
Never: "she said sarcastically." Instead: write a line that drips with sarcasm.
Never: "she giggled" a sentence. Or "smiled" one. If you want a character
to smile, have her smile — as a separate action.

Exception: "whispered," "shouted," "muttered" — when the volume genuinely
matters and isn't clear from context.

Acceptable invisible verbs beyond "said": told, explained, replied, remarked,
asked. These don't attract attention. But replacing "said" with an assortment
of fancy verbs is unnecessary if you write clearly enough that the reader
knows who's speaking.

### Law 6: The Rule of Twelve

Grab any passage of great dialogue from any great novel. Count the words
between punctuation marks. You will seldom find more than twelve.

Humans speak in short bursts. Characters should do the same. Use twelve
as a maximum for most exchanges. Aim for half that to keep dialogue
terse and crisp. Longer phrases and clauses in dialogue should be shortened
unless the character's voice specifically demands extended syntax (a professor,
a preacher, someone performing authority through verbosity).

### Law 7: Desire Drives Speech

When characters speak, they should be trying to get something from each
other, or make a power play. Every line of dialogue should be motivated by
a want.

Ask: what does this character want RIGHT NOW in this exchange?
Will they be blunt or subtly manipulative?
Will they be angry, or do they always keep their cool?
Does the desire shift during the conversation?

Characters who speak without wanting anything produce dialogue that reads
like weather — present but functionless.

## Part II: Nine Tension Elements for Dialogue

Tension in dialogue comes from the gap between expectations and reality.
Figure out what your character expects from a conversation, then block them.

### Element 1: Personality Clashes

A fixer who analyzes quickly is talking to someone with a rambling, disorganized
mind. She cuts him off. He gets defensive. The clash isn't about the topic —
it's about incompatible operating systems. Build your cast with personality
conflict in mind and the tension mines set themselves.

### Element 2: Opposing Conversational Goals

Beyond story goals, characters have conversational goals — often subconscious.
One wants to be heard and appreciated. The other wants to prove they're right.
Each steers the exchange toward their goal. Both are thwarted. When even
small goals are threatened, emotions ignite.

### Element 3: Pre-Loaded Emotions

A character enters a conversation already upset about something unrelated,
then bites the other person's head off. This is universal. Pile on emotional
baggage just before an interaction, then watch the sparks.

### Element 4: Bias

A character enters the conversation expecting it to go badly — and that
expectation taints everything. They read into neutral statements. They
hear insults that weren't delivered. They brace for a fight that the other
person isn't offering. Ask: what bias does this character bring that could
poison the exchange before it starts?

### Element 5: Insecurities

Characters are sensitive to specific comments or tones. They read unintended
meaning into harmless banter. What are your character's insecurities — in
general, and regarding THIS person or situation? How might those insecurities
transform neutral dialogue into a wound?

### Element 6: Assumptions

Incorrect assumptions are the engine of miscommunication. A character assumes
the other person knows something they don't. Or believes they share an opinion
when they hold the opposite view. Or takes for granted a fact that turns out to
be wrong. The conversation built on that assumption proceeds normally — until
the foundation gives way.

### Element 7: Small Annoyances

A conversation that starts pleasant can deteriorate through accumulated
friction. The other person interrupts constantly. Checks their phone. Talks
with their mouth full. Mispronounces a word that matters. One annoyance
is tolerable. Three is a pressure cooker. Five is a confrontation.

### Element 8: Cultural Differences

Culture determines what's acceptable in conversation — gesture, eye contact,
word choice, personal space, directness. A character's ignorance of these
norms can destroy a business deal, end a romance, or blow a cover.
This element is especially potent in multi-cultural casts and any scene
involving deception across cultural lines.

### Element 9: Active Subtext

What is actually being said vs. what is meant. This is the most powerful
tension element because it operates below the surface of every line.
When you figure out what your character really thinks but won't say,
every line of spoken dialogue becomes a calculated performance — and
the reader watches for the cracks.

## Part III: Advanced Dialogue Techniques

### Technique 1: Use an Outsider for Exposition

Instead of having a character state their own qualities ("I avoid conflict"),
have another character deliver the information ("He really isn't the conflict
resolution type"). An outsider introducing information creates legitimacy.
The information feels like established fact rather than self-narration.

### Technique 2: Write Between the Lines

The best dialogue occurs when characters avoid the truth. Not through
outright lies (though those work too) but through indirection, deflection,
and coded language.

When the viewer/reader has prior knowledge the characters don't share,
watching someone misrepresent their feelings or hide their intent becomes
electric. The audience is in on the deception. This makes them complicit
and invested.

A character who wants to say "don't get in my way" cannot say it directly —
at least not until the scene reaches its breaking point. The entire exchange
is the negotiation around that unsaid sentence.

### Technique 3: Complete Characters Speak Organically

A fully realized character doesn't need dialogue rules because their motivation
generates the right words. Build the character completely — their history,
their fears, their contradictions — and dialogue becomes a natural output,
not a construction.

Your hero can be a grouch. Your villain can be polite. Actions reveal true
nature. Dialogue is the mask. The gap between the two is where character lives.

### Technique 4: Avoid Redundancy — Make It Work With One Line

If a character says the same thing four different ways, the audience understands
after the first. The redundancy is padding. Cut three lines. Keep the strongest one.

"I didn't even know the man, I only heard his name mentioned in passing,
I wouldn't know, you'd have to know the particulars—"

The point is: you don't know the guy. Make it work with one line:
"I didn't even know the man."

A story is a train on the tracks. It doesn't reverse unless there's new
information. Once you've said something — move on.

### Technique 5: Stretch Important Information

Having critical information to convey doesn't mean showing all cards immediately.
Lead the viewer/reader with breadcrumbs. Small hints about relationships.
Fragments of backstory. Each individual line works on its own. But pieced
together across the scene, they reveal the full picture.

Single-serving information stretches the effectiveness of your ultimate point.
The reader works for the revelation — and values it more because they earned it.

### Technique 6: Arrive Late, Leave Early

Skip the pleasantries. If two characters are meeting for arbitration, you don't
need the clerk unlocking the room, setting up chairs, filling water glasses.
Start at the fight. End before the handshake.

Dialogue is an extension of scenes. If the scene is trudging through boring
territory, the accompanying dialogue won't be better. Show the interesting
stuff — and stay on the interesting stuff. Don't cut away from a critical
moment to "later that night."

### Technique 7: Logical Conflict Over Manufactured Conflict

Conflict is different from arguing. Two characters can be in deep conflict
without raising their voices. A struggling screenwriter whose amateur brother
gets a script sold — that's conflict. Love and resentment in the same exchange.
The dialogue writes itself because the situation is inherently pressurized.

If you're scanning your script looking for scenes to inject conflict into,
you're in trouble. Conflict should be baked into character traits, relationships,
and competing desires. Not applied like seasoning after the fact.

### Technique 8: Strategic Interruptions

A conversation interrupted by a third party creates instant pressure. If
you're telling a secret and someone walks up — that's a compromising
situation that forces real-time decision-making.

Even if the interruptor immediately asks for a private word, you've created
a moment of visible political calculation. The reader watches the character
navigate the interruption — and what they do reveals who they are.

### Technique 9: Every Role Matters

No throwaway characters with throwaway lines. If a character exists only
to deliver generic dialogue, they should be cut.

The Die Hard principle: A businessman on a plane gives advice about
making fists with your toes after a flight. This character never returns.
But his dialogue has a point of view, delivers useful information, AND
creates the plot condition (McClane removes his shoes) that drives
a major action sequence. One scene. Three functions.

### Technique 10: Earn Your Speeches

Characters can deliver long speeches — but they must earn the moment.
Speeches work at the beginning or end of scenes, and at the beginning
and end of the story. They should be reserved for the most important
characters. The speech should be the culmination of pressure, not
a substitute for it.

### Technique 11: Consistency Until the Break

Characters should be tonally consistent in their speech — until a major
story event forces them to change. The polite character who finally explodes.
The quiet one who delivers the devastating line. Coordinate tonal shifts
with act breaks and turning points. The shift itself becomes a moment.

### Technique 12: Foreshadowing and Callbacks

A detail mentioned in dialogue early in the story pays off later.
Callbacks are proof that the story has a design — that what seemed
casual was actually structural.

The best callbacks are set up by dialogue that seemed innocuous at the
time but returns at a critical moment with full force. This works for
drama and comedy equally.

Plant wide. Pay off precise.

### Technique 13: Relationships Alter Speech

People speak differently depending on who they're talking to. To a mother —
one way. To friends — another. To authority — another still.

When everyone speaks to the king one way but the hero speaks to him
differently, it reveals character without exposition. The deviation from
the established speech pattern IS the characterization.

### Technique 14: The Rubber Ducky Problem

Avoid having a character explain their entire psychology through a single
traumatic backstory. "Someone took his rubber ducky away from him, and
that's why he's a deranged killer."

Behavior should reveal psychology. Not the other way around. The reader
should understand the character's damage from how they ACT — the
explanation, if it comes at all, should confirm what the reader already suspected.

### Technique 15: Read It Aloud

The final audit. Read every line of dialogue aloud. Lines that look good
on the page often sound wrong spoken. Sometimes the fix is a contraction.
Sometimes it's a complete rewrite. Your ear knows things your eye misses.

## Part IV: Formatting and Mechanics

### Attribution Placement

- Before the line: Sets up the speaker. "Marcus leaned back. 'That's not what I said.'"
- After the line: Reveals the speaker. "'That's not what I said.' Marcus leaned back."
- Mid-line: Creates a pause. "'That's not,' Marcus said, 'what I said.'"
- Omitted: When context makes the speaker obvious. Don't tag every line in a two-person exchange.

### Action Beats vs. Dialogue Tags

Action beats (character does something between lines) are stronger than tags
for showing emotion and maintaining scene grounding.

Weak: "'I don't believe you,' she said suspiciously."
Strong: "'I don't believe you.' She set the glass down without drinking."

The action beat shows the suspicion through behavior. The reader constructs
the emotion. This is always more powerful than being told.

### Paragraph Breaks

New speaker = new paragraph. Always. No exceptions.

### Silence and Interruption

- **Silence**: Describe what happens in the silence. The absence of speech IS dialogue.
- **Interruption**: Em dash at the break point. "I was going to tell you, but—" / "You weren't going to tell me anything."
- **Trailing off**: Ellipsis. "I thought we could... never mind."
- **Pause**: Action beat between lines. She stared at the wall for a long time. "Okay."

### Show, Don't Tell — Emotion in Dialogue

The adverb shortcut: "Get it," he said angrily.
The better version: "Get the damn thing," he said.
The best version:

He slapped his palm on the table with a force that rattled the plates,
but when he spoke she had to lean forward to hear him. "Get it now."

The last version has ominous tone. The reader recognizes anger in the
character's actions. No telling necessary. Showing will never be as easy
as telling. But your goal is to put readers close enough to feel the table shake.

## Part V: Dialogue Anti-Patterns

### The Exposition Dump

Characters telling each other things they both already know for the reader's benefit.
"As you know, Dr. Chen, the reactor has been unstable since the incident last March."

**Fix**: Characters discuss consequences, disagreements, or next steps about
known events — not the events themselves. Deliver information bit by bit.
Allow readers to draw their own conclusions. Trust the reader.

Bad: "Do you remember mother's last boyfriend, Jack Smart, who sold medical
equipment in British Columbia, until he was charged with fraud, and who had
a daughter Jackie, who studied at Yale?"

Better: Break it into pieces across multiple exchanges. Let the information
arrive through argument, humor, or reaction — not recitation.

### The Identical Voice

All characters speak with the same vocabulary, sentence length, and rhythm.

**Fix**: Build a voice card for each character before writing. Minimum 5
distinguishing characteristics. Cover the names — if you can't tell who's
talking, rewrite.

### The Tennis Match

Alternating lines of equal length with no interruption, overlap, or silence.

**Fix**: Vary. One character dominates for 3 lines. The other responds with
one word. Silence. Then the power shifts.

### The Speechifier

A character delivers a monologue that sounds like an essay.

**Fix**: If a character must speak at length, have another character interrupt,
react, or physically do something during the speech. Break the monologue
with scene.

### The On-the-Nose Dialogue

Characters state exactly what they feel.
"I'm angry at you because you betrayed my trust."

**Fix**: "Don't come to the house anymore." (The reader infers the anger
and the reason.)

### The Name Abuser

Characters overuse each other's names in dialogue. "Thank you, Charlie.
I appreciate that. By the way, Charlie, now that I have you here, what do
you think?"

Overuse of a name reads as insincere or manipulative (unless that's the
point). Once per exchange is the ceiling for natural dialogue.

### The Filler Addict

"Um," "uh," "like," "uh huh" — realistic but lethal in fiction. These words
remind the reader they're reading. Cut them. The dialogue should feel
real without the detritus of actual speech.

### The Modern-Language Failure

"My dear Mr. Bennet" worked in 1813. Avoid archaic language unless the
character specifically demands it — and even then, use it sparingly. The
occasional archaic word characterizes. Too many make the reader think
about the words instead of the story.

## Part VI: Dialogue in Thriller/Suspense Contexts

### Interrogation Dialogue

See `references/compiler/chapter_construction.md` for full doctrine.

Key principles:
- Every question from the antagonist operates on at least two layers (surface inquiry + probe)
- The protagonist's answers must be calibrated to reveal nothing while appearing open
- Silence from the antagonist after an answer is more threatening than a follow-up question
- The fatal slip is always small — a gesture, an accent, a cultural error — never a confession

### Dialogue as Power Map

Track who controls the conversation beat by beat. Control = asking questions,
changing topics, and determining when silence falls. The character asking
questions holds power. The moment the questioned character asks a question
back, the power dynamic is contested.

### The Weaponized Pleasantry

The most dangerous dialogue in thrillers sounds polite. "Have you tried
the strudel?" is more terrifying than "I know who you are" — because
the first forces the protagonist to perform normalcy while the ground
shifts beneath them.

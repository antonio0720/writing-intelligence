# Genre Pack: Dialogue

## Weighting Adjustments

| Category | Weight Modifier | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Voice Integrity | +5 | Each character must sound distinct |
| Rhythm | +5 | Dialogue IS rhythm — pace, interruption, silence |
| Memorability | +5 | Great dialogue lines persist in culture |
| Density | +3 | People don't speak in paragraphs |
| Evidence Discipline | -8 | Replaced by character consistency and subtext |
| Structural Control | -3 | Dialogue follows emotional logic, not section architecture |

## Core Laws

### Law 1: Every Character Has a Voice Fingerprint
If you cover the attribution tags and can't tell who's speaking, the dialogue
has failed. Distinction comes from:

- **Vocabulary tier**: A hedge fund manager and a mechanic don't use the same words
- **Sentence length**: Some people speak in fragments. Others in paragraphs. Most are somewhere specific.
- **Verbal habits**: Repeated phrases, filler words, pet expressions
- **What they DON'T say**: Evasion patterns, topics they avoid, emotions they suppress
- **Rhythm**: Staccato vs. flowing. Precise vs. rambling. Direct vs. circumlocutory.

### Law 2: Dialogue is Not Conversation
Real conversation is full of filler, repetition, and dead ends. Dialogue is
conversation with purpose — every exchange advances character, plot, or theme.
The craft is making it FEEL real while being compressed.

### Law 3: Subtext Over Text
People rarely say what they mean. They say something adjacent and let the
listener decode it. The gap between what's said and what's meant is where
dialogue gets its power.

"Are you coming to dinner?" means: "Are we still together?"
"I'm fine" means: "I am not fine and I need you to notice."
"That's an interesting approach" means: "That's wrong."

### Law 4: Conflict is the Engine
Every dialogue scene needs tension. Not necessarily argument — tension.
Unequal information. Competing desires. Hidden agendas. Power asymmetry.
Two people who want the same thing and agree about everything do not need
a scene.

### Law 5: Said is Invisible
The reader's brain skips "said." It processes "exclaimed," "retorted,"
"opined," "queried" as speed bumps. Use "said" and "asked" for 90%+
of attribution. Let the dialogue itself carry the emotion.

Never: "he said angrily." Instead: write angry words.
Never: "she said sarcastically." Instead: write a line that drips with sarcasm.

Exception: "whispered," "shouted," "muttered" — when the volume genuinely
matters and isn't clear from context.

## Formatting

### Attribution Placement
- Before the line: Sets up the speaker. "Marcus leaned back. 'That's not what I said.'"
- After the line: Reveals the speaker. "'That's not what I said.' Marcus leaned back."
- Mid-line: Creates a pause. "'That's not,' Marcus said, 'what I said.'"
- Omitted: When context makes the speaker obvious. Don't tag every line in a two-person exchange.

### Action Beats vs. Dialogue Tags
Action beats (character does something between lines) are stronger than tags
for showing emotion and maintaining scene grounding.

Weak: "'I don't believe you,' she said suspiciously."
Strong: "'I don't believe you.' She set the glass down without drinking."

### Paragraph Breaks
New speaker = new paragraph. Always. No exceptions.

### Silence and Interruption
- Silence: Describe what happens in the silence. The absence of speech IS dialogue.
- Interruption: Em dash at the break point. "I was going to tell you, but—" / "You weren't going to tell me anything."
- Trailing off: Ellipsis. "I thought we could... never mind."
- Pause: Action beat between lines. She stared at the wall for a long time. "Okay."

## Dialogue Anti-Patterns

### The Exposition Dump
Characters telling each other things they both already know for the reader's benefit.
"As you know, Dr. Chen, the reactor has been unstable since the incident last March."

Fix: Characters discuss consequences, disagreements, or next steps about known events — not the events themselves.

### The Identical Voice
All characters speak with the same vocabulary, sentence length, and rhythm.

Fix: Build a voice card for each character before writing. Minimum 5 distinguishing characteristics.

### The Tennis Match
Alternating lines of equal length with no interruption, overlap, or silence.

Fix: Vary. One character dominates for 3 lines. The other responds with one word. Silence. Then the power shifts.

### The Speechifier
A character delivers a monologue that sounds like an essay.

Fix: If a character must speak at length, have another character interrupt, react, or physically do something during the speech. Break the monologue with scene.

### The On-the-Nose Dialogue
Characters state exactly what they feel.
"I'm angry at you because you betrayed my trust."

Fix: "Don't come to the house anymore." (The reader infers the anger and the reason.)

# Genre Pack: Legal Positioning

## Weighting Adjustments

| Category | Weight Modifier | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Clarity | +5 | Ambiguity in legal prose creates liability |
| Specificity | +5 | Every claim must be defensible and precise |
| Evidence Discipline | +5 | Unsupported claims are malpractice-adjacent |
| Argument Strength | +5 | The argument must survive adversarial scrutiny |
| Rhythm | -5 | Function over form |
| Memorability | -5 | Legal prose aims for precision, not quotability |

## Architecture

### Position Papers / Legal Memoranda
1. **Issue**: State the legal question in one sentence
2. **Short Answer**: State the conclusion in 2-3 sentences
3. **Facts**: Relevant facts stated neutrally. No characterization.
4. **Analysis**: Rule → Application → Conclusion for each sub-issue
   - State the legal rule with citation
   - Apply the rule to the specific facts
   - Draw the conclusion
5. **Counterarguments**: Address the strongest opposing position
6. **Conclusion**: Restate the position with the key supporting rationale

### Demand Letters / Advocacy Prose
1. **Identification**: Who you represent, what this concerns
2. **Facts**: Stated in narrative form with strategic emphasis
3. **Legal Framework**: Applicable law, precedent, regulatory authority
4. **Application**: How the law applies to these facts
5. **Demand/Position**: Specific action requested, timeline, consequences of non-compliance

## Voice Notes

- Precision is the primary virtue. "On or about March 14, 2025" not "recently."
- Passive voice is acceptable when the actor is unknown or strategically de-emphasized.
  "The payment was not received" (focus on the failure). "Defendant failed to remit
  payment" (focus on the actor).
- Hedging is strategic, not cowardly. "It appears that" signals genuine uncertainty.
  "It is our position that" signals advocacy awareness.
- Defined terms use initial capitals after first definition: "the Agreement," "the Property," "the Parties."
- Shall vs. Will vs. Must: "Shall" creates obligation. "Will" states future fact.
  "Must" is clearer than "shall" in modern drafting. Know which your jurisdiction prefers.
- Citations follow jurisdiction-specific format. Always.

## Precision Rules

- "And/or" — avoid. State the logical relationship explicitly.
- "Reasonable" — define what reasonable means in context. "Within 30 days" beats "within a reasonable time."
- "Material" — define the threshold. "$10,000 or 5% of contract value, whichever is greater" beats "a material amount."
- "Including but not limited to" — use when the list is illustrative. "Including" alone may be construed as exhaustive in some jurisdictions.
- "Best efforts" vs. "Reasonable efforts" vs. "Commercially reasonable efforts" — these are different standards. Use the one you mean.

## Common Failures

- Conclusory statements without legal analysis ("This clearly violates...")
- Citing cases without explaining their relevance to the facts
- Emotional language in analytical sections
- Failing to address adverse authority
- Ambiguous pronoun references (fatal in contracts)
- Using "feel" or "believe" where "contend" or "assert" is appropriate
- Mixing advocacy and analysis in the same section

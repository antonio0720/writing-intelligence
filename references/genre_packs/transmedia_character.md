# Genre Pack: Transmedia Character Deepening v1.0

## Weighting Adjustments

| Category | Weight Modifier | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Voice Integrity | +8 | Character voice must survive translation across media formats |
| Specificity | +5 | Abstract characters collapse when transported to new formats |
| Audience Fit | +5 | Each format has a different audience relationship |

## I. Purpose and Doctrine

### Why Transmedia Character Content Exists

Characters that live only inside their primary text are finite. Characters that
extend across formats — written diaries, audio dramas, interactive serials,
visual media, guided experiences — become ecosystems. The reader doesn't
just consume the story. They inhabit it.

**Transmedia character content must**:
- Deepen the character without contradicting the primary text
- Provide value that is inaccessible within the primary format
- Stand alone as compelling content even for newcomers
- Reward existing readers with insider knowledge, emotional resonance, or narrative expansion
- Be production-viable (scope matched to resources)

### The Sovereignty Principle

The primary text is sovereign. All transmedia content serves the primary
narrative. No transmedia piece may contradict, retcon, or devalue events
in the primary text. Transmedia content expands the universe laterally
(side stories, unseen moments, deeper lore) — not vertically (overwriting
what the primary text established).

## II. Written Transmedia Formats

### Character Diaries

**What they are**: Weekly or serialized first-person entries written from a
character's perspective, covering their internal world — thoughts, fears,
private moments, and reactions to events in the primary narrative.

**Character selection criteria**:
- Rich inner life that the primary text can't fully explore (action-heavy primary text leaves interiority on the table)
- Involvement in enough events to sustain regular entries
- A voice distinctive enough to carry prose on its own
- Secrets or perspectives that provide new insight into known events

**Voice construction**:
- The diary's voice must reflect the character's education, cultural background, speech patterns, and emotional range
- If the character is a soldier, the diary reads like a soldier's journal — clipped, concrete, uncomfortable with abstraction
- If the character is a scholar, the diary reads like marginalia — allusive, cross-referencing, tangential
- The diary should contain moments of vulnerability the character would never show publicly

**Content architecture**:
- Each entry opens with a date or significant time marker that correlates with the primary narrative timeline
- Entries include sensory detail and emotional response (not plot summary)
- The character ponders dilemmas, celebrates, vents, lies to themselves, contradicts earlier entries
- "Unseen moments" — private reflections, hidden secrets, interactions referenced but not shown in the primary text
- At least one entry per arc should contain information that recontextualizes a scene from the primary text

**Production discipline**:
- Publishing schedule: consistent (weekly recommended), announced, maintained
- Length: 500-1500 words per entry (short enough to consume in one sitting, long enough to have substance)
- Format: clean online format, optional thematic visuals
- Audience interaction: reader comments and questions inform future entries (without ceding narrative control)

### Interactive Web Serials

**What they are**: Serialized narrative episodes published online where readers
vote on character decisions, influencing the story's direction.

**Story framework**:
- Flexible outline with key fixed events (these happen regardless of votes) and branching decision points (reader-influenced)
- The outline identifies 3-5 moments per arc where reader choice genuinely changes the trajectory
- Branching should feel consequential — if both options lead to the same outcome, the choice is fake

**Decision architecture**:
- Each episode ends at a decision point
- Decisions are binary or ternary (never more than 3 options — decision fatigue kills engagement)
- Options should be emotionally differentiated, not just tactically different ("forgive" vs. "confront" vs. "walk away" — each reveals character)
- The voted-upon outcome is revealed in the next episode's opening, with clear acknowledgment that the audience chose this

**Character consistency under branching**:
- The character's core motivations, voice, and contradictions must remain consistent regardless of which branch is taken
- Branches affect events and relationships — not the character's fundamental identity
- If a vote would break the character (e.g., voting for a pacifist to commit murder), the narrative must account for the break with appropriate psychological consequence

**Technical requirements**:
- Platform with polling/voting capability (dedicated site, social media polls, embedded forms)
- Clear voting windows with announced deadlines
- Transparency about how votes are counted and applied
- Staggered release schedule (weekly or biweekly)

### Behind-the-Scenes Lore

**What it is**: Detailed posts exploring the mythology, history, cultural
artifacts, and world-building behind the characters' universe.

**The Lore Bible**:
Before publishing any lore content, compile a master document containing:
- Complete timelines (historical and personal for each major character)
- Maps (geographical, political, social)
- Cultural norms and taboos
- Mythological systems (creation myths, prophecies, supernatural rules)
- Artifacts with provenance (who made them, what they do, where they are)
- Languages, naming conventions, and etymologies

The Lore Bible is the internal reference. Published lore posts are curated
selections from it — never the whole thing. Selective revelation maintains
mystery.

**Lore post architecture**:
- Written in engaging narrative style, not encyclopedic format
- Each post connects to a specific element in the primary text ("The weapon Kofi carries in Chapter 9 has a history that begins 400 years before he was born...")
- Structured series work well: "Artifact of the Month," "Mythology Wednesdays," "Hidden History" — gives readers a schedule and a frame
- Include visual elements: maps, sketches, photos of real-world inspirations
- End each post with a question or tease that connects to an upcoming narrative event

## III. Audio Transmedia Formats

### Podcast Series

**What it is**: A regular podcast exploring the characters' universe through
interviews (with characters voiced by actors), mythology discussions, magic/
technology system breakdowns, and thematic explorations.

**Format options**:
- **Character interviews**: An in-world journalist or historian interviews characters (voiced by actors) about events, opinions, and memories
- **Lore deep-dives**: The author discusses world-building decisions, inspirations, and hidden connections
- **Roundtable discussions**: Multiple character voices debate a thematic question from the story
- **Mixed**: Alternating formats across episodes to maintain variety

**Production requirements**:
- High-quality microphones and recording (audio is intimate — poor quality destroys the experience)
- Voice actors who embody character voices (audition for match to age, personality, emotional range)
- Theme music and consistent audio branding (intro, outro, transition sounds)
- Engaging narration — complex information delivered conversationally, not lectured
- Episode length: 20-45 minutes (short enough for a commute, long enough for depth)

### Audio Drama Series

**What it is**: Fully produced audio fiction focusing on side stories, prequels,
or parallel events that enrich the primary narrative. No visual component —
story told entirely through dialogue, sound design, and narration.

**Story selection**:
- Choose side stories that fans show interest in or that contain untold secrets
- Each series should have a complete arc (beginning, middle, end)
- The events should connect to the primary text but not require it for comprehension
- Ideal subjects: the villain's origin, an ally's mission before they met the protagonist, a historical event referenced in the primary text

**Audio-specific writing rules**:
- No visual cues available — every piece of information must come through dialogue, narration, or sound
- Scripts must be heavily dialogue-driven with strong sound cues ("The door slammed. Footsteps on wet stone. Then nothing.")
- Each character's voice must be immediately distinguishable (age, accent, rhythm, vocabulary)
- Sound design replaces scene description — ambient sounds place the listener in the environment

**Production architecture**:
- Professional voice actors (diverse casting matching character demographics)
- Layered sound design: ambient environment, relevant effects, thematic music
- Balanced audio mix: voice clear above background, effects punctuating not overwhelming
- Episode length: 15-30 minutes
- Release schedule: consistent (weekly or biweekly during a season)

### Guided Meditations

**What they are**: Meditation sessions inspired by the spiritual, philosophical,
or psychological practices within the story world. Each session helps listeners
find inner peace or strength through the same techniques the characters use.

**When this format works**:
- The story contains spiritual practices, mentor-student relationships, or psychological disciplines
- Characters learn techniques for centering, focusing, or transforming their mental state
- The story's philosophical framework has real-world applicability

**Construction**:
- Session structure: introduction (connect to character/theme) → guided breathing → progressive relaxation → core visualization (using story-world imagery) → reflection → gentle return
- Visualization uses symbols and settings from the story as focal points (a forest from the narrative, a significant artifact, a mentor's words)
- Character lessons or quotes woven in as reflective prompts
- Voice selection: calm, authoritative, warm — should evoke the mentor figure from the story
- Background: gentle instrumental music or nature sounds relevant to the story's setting
- Duration: 10-20 minutes per session
- Post-session: journaling prompts connecting the meditation theme to the listener's life

## IV. Visual Transmedia Formats

### Animated Shorts

**What they are**: Short animations (1-5 minutes) illustrating key moments
from the characters' lives or pivotal scenes that benefit from visual
representation.

**Scene selection criteria**:
- Visually dynamic (action, transformation, revelation)
- Emotionally concentrated (the audience should feel something in under 5 minutes)
- Self-contained (comprehensible without having read the primary text, but richer for those who have)

**Production pipeline**:
1. Script (concise, dialogue-light, action-heavy for animation)
2. Storyboard (camera angles, character movements, transitions)
3. Art direction (character designs faithful to text, color palettes matching tone)
4. Animation (fluid movement, emotional expression, timing)
5. Voice acting (casting for authenticity, recording under professional conditions)
6. Sound design (music, effects, ambient — the audio layer is 50% of animation's impact)
7. Post-production (color correction, timing adjustments, quality review)

### Character Vlogs

**What they are**: Video content where a character (you in costume, a cosplayer,
or a digital avatar) addresses the camera directly — recounting adventures,
offering advice, reflecting on events.

**Tone calibration**: The vlog should feel like eavesdropping on the character's
private thoughts delivered to a trusted friend. Not a performance. Not a lecture.
An intimate, direct-address monologue.

**Content types**:
- Adventure recaps from the character's emotional perspective (not plot summary — reaction)
- Advice segments where the character applies their story-world wisdom to real-world situations
- Confessional entries where the character reveals fears, doubts, or secrets not in the primary text
- Reaction to events (the character responds to something that happened in the latest chapter)

### Virtual Tours

**What they are**: Immersive videos showing locations from the characters'
world — using CGI, relevant real-world locations, or hybrid approaches.

**Location selection**: Choose locations that are significant to the narrative
and visually compelling. The character's home. A battlefield. A sacred site.
The location should tell a story even without narration.

**Construction**:
- Narrative voiceover providing context and character-relevant stories about each location
- 360-degree capture where possible (allows viewer agency)
- Sound design: ambient sounds specific to the location (wildlife, weather, human activity)
- Easter eggs: hidden details that reward close observation (a character's initials carved in a wall, an artifact placed in a corner)

## V. Interactive/Engagement Formats

### Digital Scavenger Hunts

**What they are**: Multi-platform puzzle experiences where participants follow
clues across websites, social media, and other digital locations to solve
mysteries related to the characters' world.

**Design architecture**:
- **Narrative wrapper**: A mini-story justifying the hunt (the character needs help finding something, a mystery needs solving, a map must be decoded)
- **Clue chain**: 5-10 clues, each leading to the next, distributed across platforms (website, Twitter/X, Instagram, email, YouTube)
- **Difficulty curve**: Early clues accessible, middle clues challenging, final clue requires synthesizing information from multiple earlier solutions
- **Integration with lore**: Clues and riddles draw on story-world knowledge, rewarding readers who know the material
- **Character presence**: Characters "host" different clue locations or appear in videos providing hints

**Incentive structure**:
- Completion rewards: exclusive content (unreleased chapter, character art, signed material)
- Participation rewards: even partial completion unlocks something
- Community element: encourage collaboration (some clues may require multiple people)

### Augmented Reality Experiences

**What they are**: AR app-based experiences that overlay story-world elements
onto the real world through a phone's camera.

**Feature set**:
- 3D character models that appear in the user's environment
- Hidden lore elements tied to real-world locations (geolocation triggers)
- Interactive story missions where the user participates in the narrative
- Collectible digital artifacts that unlock exclusive content

**Design principles**:
- AR elements must be stylistically consistent with the story's visual aesthetic
- Interactions should feel like discovery, not tutorial (the user explores, the app rewards)
- Content should change based on location, time of day, or progression — creating replay value
- Not all content available immediately — staged unlocks maintain engagement over time

## VI. Cross-Format Consistency Rules

### The Voice Lock

A character's voice must remain consistent across all formats. The diary entry,
the audio drama dialogue, the vlog monologue, and the primary text should
all be recognizably the same person. Voice is the anchor.

**Voice audit across formats**: Take one paragraph from each format featuring
the same character. Place them side by side. If any paragraph could not have
been written/spoken by that character, it breaks continuity.

### The Canon Hierarchy

1. **Primary text**: Sovereign. Cannot be contradicted.
2. **Author-produced transmedia**: Canon unless explicitly marked as "alternate" or "what-if"
3. **Interactive content**: Canon for the chosen path; unchosen paths are possibilities, not facts
4. **Fan-influenced content**: Semi-canon — it happened, but details may be adjusted if they conflict with future primary text developments

### The No-Spoiler Architecture

Transmedia content should enrich the primary text, not replace it. A reader who
consumes only the primary text should have a complete experience. Transmedia
adds depth, not dependency.

**Rule**: No plot-critical information should ONLY be available in transmedia.
Transmedia reveals what the primary text hints at. It expands what the primary
text compresses. It never contains the only copy of something essential.

### The Parallel Revenue Principle

Each transmedia format should be evaluable as a standalone product. The
podcast should be a good podcast. The diary should be good writing. The
AR app should be a compelling experience. "It connects to the story" is
not sufficient justification for low quality.

## VII. Production Prioritization

### The Effort-Impact Matrix

Not every format is appropriate for every story or every resource level.
Prioritize based on production cost vs. audience impact:

| Format | Production Cost | Audience Impact | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Character Diaries | Low (writing only) | High (intimate, recurring) | Any story with rich characters |
| Behind-the-Scenes Lore | Low-Medium (research + writing) | Medium-High (builds world) | Complex worlds with deep mythology |
| Podcast Series | Medium (recording + editing) | High (accessible, personal) | Stories with strong voices and lore |
| Interactive Web Serial | Medium (writing + platform) | High (engagement, community) | Stories with active fan communities |
| Audio Drama | High (actors + production) | Very High (immersive) | Stories with compelling side narratives |
| Animated Shorts | Very High (animation pipeline) | Very High (shareable, visual) | Stories with visually dynamic moments |
| Character Vlogs | Medium (costume + filming) | Medium (niche but loyal) | Stories with charismatic characters |
| Virtual Tours | High (CGI or location filming) | Medium-High (novelty) | Stories with iconic, detailed settings |
| Digital Scavenger Hunts | Medium (design + multi-platform) | High (event-driven engagement) | Book launches, season finales, reveals |
| AR Experiences | Very High (app development) | Variable (tech-dependent) | Major IPs with dedicated audience |

### The Launch Sequence

1. **Foundation**: Character diaries + lore posts (low cost, establishes the transmedia presence)
2. **Expansion**: Podcast + interactive serial (medium cost, builds community and recurring engagement)
3. **Immersion**: Audio drama + animated shorts (high cost, high impact, requires established audience)
4. **Event**: Scavenger hunts + AR (event-driven, tied to major releases or milestones)

| Production quality | 10 | Professional execution in the chosen format? |
| Audience engagement design | 10 | Interactive elements, community hooks? |
| Schedule consistency | 5 | Regular publishing maintained? |

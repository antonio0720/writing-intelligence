# Genre Pack: Academic Writing

## Weighting Adjustments

| Category | Weight Modifier | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Evidence Discipline | +5 | Claims without evidence fail peer review and rubrics |
| Argument Strength | +5 | The thesis must survive scrutiny |
| Clarity | +3 | Complexity of thought ≠ complexity of expression |
| Structural Control | +3 | Each section must perform a declared function |
| Memorability | -5 | Academic writing earns respect through rigor, not quotability |
| Rhythm | -3 | Clarity over musicality; but monotony still penalized |

## Architecture

### Standard Essay
1. Introduction: Hook → Context → Thesis → Roadmap (optional for shorter papers)
2. Body sections: Each organized as Claim → Evidence → Interpretation → Transition
3. Counterargument section: Steelman the opposition → Concede what's true → Pivot to why your position holds
4. Conclusion: Synthesis → Implication → Earned question

### Research Paper
1. Abstract: Problem → Method → Key Finding → Significance (one paragraph)
2. Introduction: Research gap → Research question → Significance → Thesis
3. Literature Review: Organized thematically, not chronologically
4. Method: Approach → Justification → Limitations
5. Results/Analysis: Data organized by sub-question or theme
6. Discussion: Interpretation → Comparison to existing literature → Limitations → Implications
7. Conclusion: Summary finding → Broader significance → Future directions

## Voice Notes

- Confidence comes from evidence, not from declarative force
- Qualify claims honestly: "suggests" when the evidence suggests, "demonstrates" when it demonstrates
- Use discipline-specific terminology precisely — define on first use for mixed audiences
- Avoid first person unless the discipline/professor permits it
- Signal phrases vary: "argues," "demonstrates," "contends," "observes," "notes" — each carries different weight

## Anti-Detection Specifics for Academic Writing

Academic writing is the highest-risk context for AI detection. These additional rules apply:

1. **Use course-specific language**: Terms from lectures, assigned readings, class discussions
2. **Reference specific assigned texts by author and argument, not just title**
3. **Include at least one observation that could only come from attending the class**
4. **Engage with specific arguments from sources, not just their conclusions**
5. **Show the mess**: Include one genuine qualification, one honest uncertainty, one "I'm not sure but..." moment (if first-person is permitted). Real students have uncertain moments. Perfect certainty is a tell.
6. **Vary evidence integration**: Some paraphrased, some short-quoted, some long-quoted with analysis. Uniform integration style is a pattern.
7. **Imperfect transitions**: One transition per paper can be slightly rough. Not wrong — just human. Real students don't nail every pivot.

## Common Failures

- Thesis that states a topic instead of a position
- Body paragraphs that are evidence dumps without interpretation
- Counterargument that is strawmanned or absent
- Conclusion that restates the introduction verbatim
- Citations clustered in one section, absent in others
- Block quotes without preceding introduction or following analysis
- "Since the dawn of time" or any universal historical framing

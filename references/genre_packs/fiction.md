## I. Core Principles

### Show, Don't Declare

The character's hand shakes. You don't write "she was nervous."
The rain hits the windshield in sheets. You don't write "the weather was bad."
The reader constructs the emotion from the sensory evidence.

**Exception**: Interior monologue can declare directly when the POV character
would think in those terms.

**The deeper rule**: Showing is not just about avoiding emotion-labels. It is about
trusting the reader to assemble meaning from evidence. The writer provides
observations. The reader provides interpretation. This collaboration is what
makes fiction feel alive.

He slapped his palm on the table with a force that rattled the plates, but
when he spoke she had to lean forward to hear him. "Get it now."

The reader constructs the anger, the menace, the controlled violence. No
label needed. The reader's construction is always more powerful than the
writer's declaration — because the reader built it themselves.

### Scene Over Summary

Default to scene. A scene has: a specific time, a specific place, at least
two characters (or a character and an environment), and something that changes.
Summary connects scenes. Summary is never the primary mode.

**When summary earns its place**:
- Compressing time between scenes (days pass, seasons turn)
- Establishing pattern before breaking it ("Every Tuesday he walked the same route." — until the Tuesday he didn't)
- Accelerating through events whose details don't matter (the drive to the airport, not the drive itself)
- Montage rhythm — parallel fragments showing the same action across different contexts

**When summary is a failure**:
- Summarizing an event that should have been a scene (the fight happened off-page)
- Telling the reader what a character felt during a moment that deserved dramatization
- Reporting dialogue instead of rendering it ("She told him about her mother's death" — show us that conversation)

### Detail Selection

Not every detail. The RIGHT detail. One precise observation beats five
generic ones.

"She wore a red dress" = nothing.
"She wore the dress she'd bought for the interview she didn't get" = character, history, emotional state.

**The specific detail doctrine**:
- Details should be selected for what they reveal about character, mood, or theme — not for photographic completeness
- A room described with three precise details is more vivid than a room described with fifteen generic ones
- The detail the character notices tells the reader what the character is thinking about
- Physical descriptions that read like police reports ("5'10", brown hair, medium build") are dead prose. Select the ONE detail a stranger would notice first — and let that detail carry the characterization

### The Right Detail Test

For every descriptive detail, ask:
1. Does this detail reveal character, mood, or theme?
2. Would a different detail do the job better?
3. Does this detail do ONLY description, or does it do description PLUS something else?
4. If I cut this detail, does the reader lose anything they need?

If the answer to #3 is "only description," the detail is decorative. Replace it
with one that works harder.

## II. Scene Architecture (v2.0)

### Every Scene Has a Turn

A scene without a turn — a moment where something changes — is a scene
that doesn't need to exist. The turn doesn't have to be dramatic. It can be
a realization, a shifted relationship, a piece of information, a decision made.
But the characters and the reader must exit the scene in a different state
than they entered it.

**Scene audit**: What changed between the first line and the last? If the
answer is "nothing," the scene is exposition in costume.

### Setting as Active Force

Read `references/compiler/chapter_construction.md` for full doctrine.

Settings are not backdrops. They are participants. The weather, the space,
the architecture, the ambient sound — these elements should pressure the
characters, reflect their psychology, or contradict their words.

**The Pathetic Fallacy Problem**: Rain = sadness is a cliché. But the solution
is not to abandon environment-as-participant. The solution is to use it with
more precision. The rain doesn't mirror the character's sadness. The rain
forces them to take shelter in the one place they were avoiding. The heat
doesn't symbolize tension. The heat makes the characters irritable, and their
irritability breaks the conversation open.

Environment should ACT on the characters, not merely reflect them.

### Props as Narrative Engines

Read `references/positive_patterns/power_dynamics.md` for full system.

Every named object in a scene should serve at least two functions. Objects
that serve only their literal function are furniture. Objects that serve literal
function plus character revelation, foreshadowing, power signaling, or
thematic argument are narrative architecture.

The glass she's drinking from. The book on the table. The photo on the wall.
The car keys on the counter. Each of these is either working or not. Audit them.

### The Cold Open

Arrive late. The reader doesn't need the character waking up, getting dressed,
driving to the location. Drop them into the scene at the moment the scene
begins to matter. Context arrives through immersion, not briefing.

The first line of a scene is an audition. If it doesn't compel the reader to
read the second line, everything after it is academic.

### The Button

The last line of a scene is what the reader carries into the white space.
It should be the most carefully chosen line in the scene. One image. One
statement. One silence. Not a summary paragraph. Not a reflection.
The button is the scar the scene leaves.

## III. Character Deployment (v2.0)

### Character as Function

Read `references/compiler/character_role_archetypes.md` for the 12-role system.

Every character in a scene serves a structural function. The question is not
"who is this person?" but "what does this person DO to the scene's tension,
information, and power dynamics?"

Characters who serve no structural function are dead weight. They should
be cut, merged with another character, or given a function.

### Voice Fingerprint

Read `references/genre_packs/dialogue.md` (v2.0) for full dialogue doctrine.

Each character must have a distinctive voice — vocabulary, sentence length,
rhythm, verbal habits, evasion patterns, what they don't say. The test:
cover the attribution tags. If you can't tell who's speaking, rewrite.

### Character Contradiction

Real people contain contradictions. The generous person who's jealous.
The brave person who's terrified of one specific thing. The honest person
who lies about one topic. Characters with clean internal consistency are
concepts, not people.

The contradiction doesn't need to be explained. It needs to be demonstrated.
The reader should notice it and be slightly uncomfortable. That discomfort
is characterization working.

### The Desire Engine

Every character wants something in every scene. Not just in the story — in
each individual scene. The desire may be large (survival, love, freedom)
or small (a drink, silence, the last word). But desire drives action, and
action drives scene.

When two characters want incompatible things — even small, incompatible
things — the scene writes itself. When characters want nothing, the scene
stalls.

## IV. Pacing Mastery (v2.0)

### Sentence Architecture as Tempo Control

| Pacing Need | Technique |
|---|---|
| **Tension building** | Sentences shorten. Paragraphs compress. White space increases between paragraphs. |
| **Action** | Staccato. Subject-verb. Subject-verb. No subordinate clauses. No commas if possible. |
| **Aftermath / reflection** | Sentences expand. Details return. Subordinate clauses reappear. The camera lingers. |
| **Suspense** | Delay the reveal. Stack observations that approach but don't arrive. The reader's brain completes the circuit one beat before the text does. |
| **Montage** | Parallel fragments. Same structure, different content. Time compresses. |
| **The held beat** | One image, extended past the reader's expectation. No plot. No dialogue. Just duration. |

### The Compression Principle

The most important moments in a story get the most words per unit of
story time. A gunfight that lasts three seconds might take a full page. A year
of marriage might take a paragraph.

**Time compression ratio**:
- Climactic moments: 1 second of story time = 100+ words of prose
- Active scenes: 1 minute of story time = 200-500 words
- Transitional passages: 1 hour to 1 day of story time = 1-3 sentences
- Summary passages: Weeks to months = 1 paragraph

The reader should never be consciously aware of these ratios. But they should
FEEL the difference — the breathless density of a critical moment vs. the
airy speed of a transition.

### Pacing Across Chapters

Read `references/compiler/chapter_construction.md` for the eight-phase compression model.

Chapters have internal pacing (sentence/paragraph level) and external pacing
(where they sit in the arc). A chapter that is internally perfectly paced but
externally misplaced (a slow reflective chapter at the climax, a frantic action
chapter during setup) will feel wrong regardless of its craft.

**Chapter pacing audit**:
- Does this chapter's tempo match its position in the story arc?
- Does the chapter's internal pacing vary, or is it one speed throughout?
- Are the fastest and slowest moments within the chapter placed where they need to be (fastest at climax, slowest at aftermath)?

## V. Time in Fiction

### Linear is Default, Not Obligation

Most stories are told chronologically. This is the easiest structure for the
reader to track and the hardest structure for the writer to make interesting.
The challenge of linear storytelling is maintaining momentum without the
structural novelty of time manipulation.

### Flashback Discipline

A flashback earns its place when — and only when — it reveals information
that changes how the reader understands the present. If the flashback is
providing backstory for context, it should probably be cut and its information
delivered through present-tense action and dialogue.

**Flashback rules**:
- Enter the flashback through a specific sensory trigger in the present (a smell, a sound, an object)
- The flashback should have its own mini-arc (setup, turn, button)
- Exit the flashback at a moment that recontextualizes the present scene
- The present scene should be different AFTER the flashback — the reader's understanding has shifted

### Parallel Timelines

If two timelines run simultaneously, they need distinct markers so the reader
always knows WHEN they are. Markers can be:
- Different tense (past/present)
- Different voice (first person/third person)
- Different narrative style (lyrical/spare)
- Different chapter naming convention
- A recurring motif that exists in both timelines but changes

The two timelines should comment on each other — thematic resonance,
ironic juxtaposition, or causal connection.

### Time Compression and Dilation

The writer controls the reader's experience of time. A conversation can last
ten pages (dilation). A war can last one sentence (compression). The skill
is knowing which events deserve which treatment.

**The rule**: What matters most gets the most time. What matters least gets
the least. This sounds obvious, but the most common pacing failure in fiction
is spending too much time on what doesn't matter and rushing through what does.

## VI. Dialogue in Fiction (v2.0)

Read `references/genre_packs/dialogue.md` (v2.0) for comprehensive doctrine.

### Essential Fiction-Dialogue Rules

- Dialogue reveals character through what is said AND what is not said
- No dialogue tag other than "said" unless the manner is genuinely unclear
- No adverbs on dialogue tags. "He said angrily" → show the anger in words or action
- People interrupt. People trail off. People lie. People say the opposite of what they mean.
- Each character has a distinct speech pattern. Cover the names — you should still know who's talking.
- Dialogue is compressed reality, not transcribed reality. Cut filler. Cut pleasantries. Cut repetition.
- The Rule of Twelve: most dialogue phrases should be under twelve words between punctuation marks

### Dialogue as Scene Engine

In fiction, dialogue does not just convey information. It performs the scene.
Every exchange should advance character, plot, or theme — ideally two or
three simultaneously.

A dialogue scene where characters exchange information but no power shifts,
no emotional change occurs, and no desire is thwarted is not a scene. It's
an info-dump wearing dialogue's clothes.

### Subtext in Fiction Dialogue

The gap between what's said and what's meant is where fiction dialogue gets
its power. The reader who decodes the subtext feels intelligent, invested,
and complicit. The reader who's told what the subtext means feels patronized.

Never have the narrator explain what the character "really meant." Trust
the reader. If the subtext is too subtle, the fix is better dialogue — not
authorial commentary.

## VII. Common Failures (v2.0)

### Prose-Level Failures
- Emotional states named instead of demonstrated
- Physical descriptions that read like police reports
- Weather as mood (unless weather does actual narrative work)
- Dreams as meaningful (they almost never earn their place)
- Adverbs carrying emotional weight that should be in the action
- Every paragraph the same length (monotone pacing)

### Scene-Level Failures
- Scenes without a turn (nothing changes)
- Settings that serve only as backdrop (the space never acts on characters)
- Props that serve only their literal function (no dual-purpose objects)
- Characters present in a scene who serve no structural function
- Violence without cost (action that changes nothing)
- The scene ends where it should have started (too much setup)

### Chapter-Level Failures
- Chapters that start slow and stay slow (no compression model)
- The fatal detail absent (no single moment that transforms the scene)
- Aftermath skipped (violence or revelation without consequence)
- Foreshadowing absent (climactic details not planted earlier)
- The button is weak (the last line doesn't carry the chapter's weight)

### Dialogue-Level Failures
- Characters who all sound the same
- Exposition delivered through dialogue no real person would speak
- On-the-nose dialogue (characters state exactly what they feel)
- The tennis match (alternating lines of equal length, no interruption)
- Dialogue without subtext (what's said is all there is)
- Characters overusing each other's names

### Character-Level Failures
- Characters without contradictions (concepts, not people)
- Characters without desire (nothing driving their actions)
- Characters whose role in the scene is unclear (structural dead weight)
- Themes stated explicitly by a character instead of embodied by the narrative
- Plots that rely on characters being stupid rather than in genuinely impossible situations

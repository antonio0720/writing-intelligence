# Genre Pack: Speeches

## Weighting Adjustments

| Category | Weight Modifier | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Rhythm | +5 | Speech is performed. Rhythm is half the meaning. |
| Memorability | +5 | The audience takes home lines, not paragraphs |
| Voice Integrity | +5 | The speaker must sound like themselves |
| Audience Fit | +3 | Wrong register = lost audience |
| Evidence Discipline | -5 | Replaced by anecdote, authority, and shared experience |
| Density | -3 | Speeches need breathing room. Silence is a tool. |

## Architecture

### Opening (30 seconds)
One of: specific moment / startling fact / direct address / bold claim.
Never: "Thank you for having me" as the first sentence. Gratitude can come second.

### Foundation (2-3 minutes)
Establish shared reality. Name what the audience is living through. Anchor in specifics they recognize.

### Argument/Narrative (5-8 minutes)
3 main points maximum. Each one: claim → story or evidence → implication.
Each point has a landing line — a single sentence that crystallizes the point.

### The Turn (1 minute)
The moment where the speech shifts from diagnosis to prescription, from problem to possibility, from observation to conviction.

### Climax (2-3 minutes)
Peak emotional and rhetorical intensity. Cadence accelerates. Sentences shorten.
Repetition structures (anaphora, epistrophe, tricolon) carry the momentum.

### Close (30-60 seconds)
One of: challenge / call to action / return to opening image / quiet declaration.
The last line is the most important line. It's what the audience carries home.

## Voice Notes

- Write for the ear, not the eye. Read every sentence aloud.
- Short sentences for emphasis. Long sentences for building.
- Contractions are natural in speech. Use them.
- Pause indicators: line breaks, em dashes (this is the one context where em dashes earn their place), and short paragraphs all signal where the speaker breathes.
- Applause architecture: the audience needs space to respond. End key lines with a falling cadence and a beat of silence (new paragraph).

## Common Failures

- Reading an essay out loud instead of delivering a speech
- No landing lines — the audience has nothing to hold onto
- Flat energy throughout — no escalation, no climax
- The "list speech" — five points of equal weight, none memorable
- Closing with "thank you" instead of a final line that resonates

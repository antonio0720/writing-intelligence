# Genre Pack: Medical Writing

## Weighting Adjustments

| Category | Weight Modifier | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Evidence Discipline | +5 | Lives depend on accuracy |
| Clarity | +5 | Misinterpretation causes harm |
| Specificity | +5 | Dosages, mechanisms, populations must be exact |
| Argument Strength | +3 | Clinical recommendations require rigorous logic |
| Density | +3 | Clinicians read under time pressure |
| Memorability | -5 | Precision over elegance |
| Rhythm | -5 | Clarity over cadence |

## Subgenres

### Clinical Research Paper (IMRAD)

1. **Introduction**: Knowledge gap → Research question → Hypothesis
2. **Methods**: Study design → Population → Intervention → Outcomes → Statistical plan
3. **Results**: Primary outcomes → Secondary outcomes → Subgroup analyses → Adverse events
4. **Discussion**: Interpretation → Comparison to literature → Limitations → Clinical implications

CONSORT (RCTs), STROBE (observational), PRISMA (systematic reviews) checklists
govern what must be reported. Know which applies.

### Case Report

1. **Introduction**: Why this case is worth reporting (novel presentation, rare condition, unexpected outcome)
2. **Case presentation**: Demographics → Presenting symptoms → Examination findings → Investigations → Diagnosis → Treatment → Outcome → Follow-up
3. **Discussion**: Differential diagnosis → Literature comparison → Mechanism → Learning points

### Clinical Practice Guideline Summary

1. **Population**: Who does this apply to?
2. **Recommendation**: What to do, with strength of recommendation (strong/conditional)
3. **Evidence quality**: GRADE rating (high/moderate/low/very low)
4. **Rationale**: Why this recommendation over alternatives
5. **Implementation**: Practical considerations for the clinician

### Patient Education Material

1. **What is this condition?** (plain language, ≤8th grade reading level)
2. **What are the symptoms?**
3. **How is it diagnosed?**
4. **What are the treatment options?**
5. **What should I expect?**
6. **When should I seek emergency care?**
7. **Where can I learn more?**

## Voice Notes

### Precision Requirements
- Drug names: Generic name first, brand in parentheses on first mention. "Metformin (Glucophage)"
- Dosing: Always include dose, route, frequency, duration. "Amoxicillin 500mg PO TID × 10 days"
- Lab values: Include units and reference ranges. "Hemoglobin 8.2 g/dL (normal: 12.0-16.0)"
- Statistics: Report exact p-values, confidence intervals, effect sizes. "HR 0.76 (95% CI: 0.63-0.92, p=0.004)"
- Populations: Specify age, sex, comorbidities, inclusion/exclusion criteria
- Timelines: "At 6-week follow-up" not "later" or "subsequently"

### Hedging Conventions
Medical hedging is not weakness — it is precision about uncertainty:
- "Associated with" (correlation, not causation established)
- "May contribute to" (mechanistically plausible but not proven)
- "Has been shown to" (in specific study populations — may not generalize)
- "In this patient population" (limiting the scope of the claim)
- "Based on limited evidence" (honest about data quality)

### Prohibited
- Causal language without RCT evidence: "causes," "prevents," "cures" (unless proven)
- Definitive statements about individual patients from population data
- Unattributed statistics
- Recommendation without evidence grading
- "Interesting" or "fascinating" as analytical terms
- Mixing correlation and causation language

## Evidence Hierarchy

| Level | Source | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| 1a | Systematic review of RCTs | Highest |
| 1b | Individual RCT | High |
| 2a | Systematic review of cohort studies | Moderate-high |
| 2b | Individual cohort study | Moderate |
| 3 | Case-control study | Moderate-low |
| 4 | Case series / case report | Low |
| 5 | Expert opinion | Lowest |

Always state the evidence level supporting each claim.

## Common Failures

- Methods section too vague to replicate the study
- Results section that interprets instead of reports (interpretation belongs in Discussion)
- Discussion section that overclaims from the data
- Limitations section that is perfunctory instead of honest
- Patient education written at college reading level
- Missing adverse event reporting
- Statistical significance reported without clinical significance
- Absolute risk presented as relative risk (or vice versa) without context
- "No significant difference" interpreted as "no difference" (absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence)

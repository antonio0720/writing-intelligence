# Genre Pack: Cinematic Narration

## Weighting Adjustments

| Category | Weight Modifier | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Rhythm | +5 | Cinematic prose is pacing. The reader should feel cuts, zooms, holds. |
| Voice Integrity | +5 | Cinematic narration demands a distinctive directorial eye |
| Memorability | +5 | The image is the argument. It must burn. |
| Specificity | +3 | Sensory precision replaces abstract commentary |
| Evidence Discipline | -8 | Replaced by verisimilitude and internal logic |
| Argument Strength | -5 | Replaced by emotional and thematic throughline |

## Core Principles

### Write in Shots
Each sentence or sentence cluster is a shot. Think in visual units.

- **Wide shot**: Sets the geography, the scope, the context. "The refinery stretched for two miles along the Houston Ship Channel, its flare stacks orange against a sky the color of wet concrete."
- **Medium shot**: Puts a character in an environment. "Vasquez stood at the edge of the catwalk, hard hat under one arm, watching the midnight shift change below."
- **Close-up**: One detail, magnified. "The scar ran from his left eyebrow to his jawline — a line drawn by someone who wanted to make sure it would never be missed."
- **Insert**: An object that carries meaning. "On the desk: a resignation letter, unsigned. The pen beside it, uncapped, dry."

### Cut, Don't Transition
Cinematic prose moves between scenes by juxtaposition, not by connector words.
A line break IS the transition. A new paragraph IS the cut.

No: "Meanwhile, across town, Marcus was dealing with his own problems."
Yes: [line break] "Marcus hadn't slept in three days."

### Pacing Through Sentence Architecture

| Pacing Need | Technique |
|---|---|
| Tension building | Sentences shorten. Paragraphs compress. White space increases. |
| Action | Staccato. Subject-verb. Subject-verb. No subordinate clauses. |
| Aftermath | Sentences expand. Details return. The camera lingers. |
| Suspense | Delay the reveal. Stack observations that approach but don't arrive. The reader's brain completes the circuit one beat before the text does. |
| Montage | Parallel fragments. Same structure, different content. Time compresses. |

### The Holding Shot
Sometimes the most powerful move is staying on one image longer than the
reader expects. The prose doesn't move. The detail accumulates. The reader
is forced to be present.

"She sat in the car for forty minutes after the engine stopped. The
windshield fogged from her breathing. The parking lot emptied around her.
The fluorescent light above her space flickered twice and went out. She
didn't move."

### Sound Design in Prose
Cinematic writing has an audio layer:
- Consonant clusters for harshness: "cracked," "struck," "wrecked"
- Long vowels for slowness: "alone," "moaning," "hollowed"
- Rhythm as soundtrack: short bursts for percussion, long sentences for strings
- Silence (white space) for the beat after the explosion

## Scene Architecture

### Cold Open
Drop the reader into the middle of action or tension. No setup. No context.
Context arrives through the scene, not before it. The reader pieces together
the situation from sensory evidence and dialogue.

### The Turn
Every scene has a moment where something changes. The power balance shifts.
Information arrives. A decision is made. A truth is revealed. If the scene
doesn't have a turn, it doesn't need to exist.

### The Button
The final line of a scene — the last image or statement before the cut.
This is the most carefully chosen element. It's what the reader carries
into the white space between scenes.

## Common Failures

- Describing emotion instead of showing the physical evidence of it
- Camera distance that never changes (staying at medium shot for the entire passage)
- Overwrought atmospheric prose without a human anchor
- Purple prose — beautiful sentences that serve the writer's vanity, not the story
- Narrating what a character thinks instead of what they do
- Explaining the symbolism instead of trusting the image
- Every paragraph the same length (uniform shot length = flat cinematography)

# Voiceprint: Academic Rigorous

## Identity

Evidence-first. Qualification-aware. Citation-dense. Writes from within a
discipline's conventions while maintaining a clear authorial position. The voice
is confident but bounded — it claims what it can prove and names what it can't.

---

## Parameters

| Dimension | Setting |
|---|---|
| Sentence aggression | Moderate — makes claims but qualifies scope. "This analysis demonstrates" not "This proves." |
| Abstraction tolerance | Medium-high — comfortable with theory but anchors every abstraction in evidence within 2 sentences. |
| Metaphor density | Very low — metaphors only when a concept genuinely lacks better expression. Prefer technical precision. |
| Emotional temperature | Cool to neutral — conviction expressed through evidence weight, not emotional language. |
| Cadence profile | Mixed — shorter claim sentences followed by longer evidential sentences with embedded citations and qualifiers. |
| Humor allowance | Near zero — occasional understated irony in footnotes or parentheticals only. |
| Directness ceiling | High for supported claims, deliberately hedged for unsupported ones. "The data suggest" not "The data prove" when the data don't prove. |
| Authority posture | Earned — authority comes from evidence and method, not from declaration. Writes as a participant in a scholarly conversation, not as a final judge. |

---

## Sentence Characteristics

- Average length: 18-24 words (longer than most voices due to embedded citations and qualifiers)
- Standard deviation: 7+ (vary with short claim sentences and long evidential ones)
- Preferred structures: Complex sentences with subordinate clauses. Passive voice acceptable when the action matters more than the actor. Active voice preferred when agency matters.
- Signal phrases: "According to [Author] ([Year])," "As [Author] argues," "[Author] demonstrates that" — varied, not repetitive.
- Hedging lexicon: "suggests," "indicates," "appears to," "may," "one possible interpretation" — used honestly, not as filler.

---

## Paragraph Characteristics

- Average length: 5-8 sentences (longer than other voices, reflecting evidence density)
- Opening: Sub-claim or topic sentence that connects to the section argument
- Body: Evidence → interpretation → evidence → interpretation (alternating, not front-loaded)
- Closing: Implication for the broader argument, connection to the next paragraph, or identification of a limit

---

## Vocabulary

- Tier: Discipline-specific terminology used precisely. Define terms on first use if writing for a mixed audience.
- Course vocabulary: Use the professor's terms, lecture frameworks, and assigned-reading language.
- Transition variety: "Moreover" and "Furthermore" no more than once each per 1000 words. Prefer structural transitions.
- Banned: "very," "really," "a lot," "things," "stuff," "kind of," "sort of," "basically"
- Required: Precise quantifiers, named studies, specific dates, page numbers when quoting

---

## Distinctive Markers

- Thesis states a position, not a topic. "This paper argues that X because Y" not "This paper examines X."
- Counterarguments addressed at their strongest, not strawmanned.
- Concessions are real — they cost the argument something.
- Sources are evaluated, not just cited. "While [Author] provides the most comprehensive dataset, their sampling method limits generalizability."
- Conclusions extend, they don't summarize. "If this reading holds, it complicates [established view] in three ways."

---

## Anti-Patterns for This Voice

- First person "I" in contexts where the discipline avoids it (check professor preference)
- Unsourced generalizations ("Many scholars believe...")
- Emotional language masquerading as analysis ("shockingly," "incredibly")
- Contractions in formal academic prose
- Starting a paragraph with a direct quote (introduce and contextualize first)
- "Since the beginning of time" or any variant of universal historical framing
- Dictionary definitions as essay openers
- Block quotes without analysis

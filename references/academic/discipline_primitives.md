# Discipline-Specific Reasoning Primitives

## Purpose

Each academic discipline has core intellectual operations — "reasoning
primitives" — that trained practitioners use automatically. Writing that
uses these primitives correctly signals real disciplinary knowledge. Writing
that uses them incorrectly or not at all signals surface-level engagement.

---

## Literature / English

### Primitives
- **Close reading**: Analyzing specific word choices, syntax, imagery, and structure
- **Symbolism**: Identifying objects, characters, or events that carry meaning beyond their literal function
- **Motif**: Tracking recurring elements across a text
- **Irony**: Identifying gaps between appearance and reality, statement and meaning
- **Tone/Diction**: How word selection creates emotional register
- **Narrative structure**: Point of view, chronology, framing, unreliable narration
- **Intertextuality**: How one text references, responds to, or subverts another
- **Ambiguity**: Recognizing and sitting with multiple valid interpretations

### What Strong Writing Looks Like
"Morrison's use of 'rememory' in *Beloved* is not a spelling error or dialect marker. It's a structural claim: memory in this novel is not something you do — it's something that exists in space, waiting. Sethe doesn't remember; she walks into a rememory that belongs to someone else."

### What Weak Writing Looks Like
"Morrison uses interesting language in her novel. The word 'rememory' is a creative choice that shows how important memory is to the story."

## History

### Primitives
- **Causation**: Distinguishing between necessary, sufficient, and contributing causes
- **Contingency**: Recognizing that outcomes were not inevitable
- **Continuity and change**: Identifying what persisted and what shifted across a period
- **Periodization**: Why certain dates/events are chosen as boundaries
- **Source evaluation**: Primary vs. secondary. Perspective. Bias. Silences.
- **Chronology**: Sequencing events with precision
- **Contextualization**: Placing events within their contemporary conditions, not modern assumptions
- **Historiography**: Understanding that historical interpretation itself has a history

### What Strong Writing Looks Like
"The standard narrative treats the Treaty of Versailles as a direct cause of World War II, but this flattens three decades of contingent decisions into a single deterministic line. Weimar inflation, Stresemann's diplomatic stabilization, the 1929 crash, Hindenburg's political miscalculation — any of these moments could have redirected the trajectory. Versailles created conditions. It did not create inevitability."

### What Weak Writing Looks Like
"The Treaty of Versailles caused World War II because Germany was punished too harshly. This made the German people angry, which led to the rise of Hitler."

## Sociology

### Primitives
- **Structure vs. agency**: Systems constrain choice; individuals act within constraints
- **Social construction**: How categories (race, gender, deviance) are created and maintained by social processes
- **Stratification**: How resources, power, and status are distributed unequally
- **Institutional analysis**: How organizations create, reproduce, and sometimes transform social patterns
- **Norms and deviance**: What behavior is expected, who defines it, and what happens when it's violated
- **Intersectionality**: How multiple axes of identity interact to produce specific experiences
- **Reflexivity**: Recognizing that the researcher's position affects what they see

### What Strong Writing Looks Like
"Mass incarceration is not simply the aggregate of individual criminal acts. It is an institution — one that produces its own labor force, creates its own disenfranchised political class, and generates multi-billion-dollar revenue streams. To analyze it only at the level of individual choices is to miss the structure that makes those choices predictable by zip code."

### What Weak Writing Looks Like
"Many people in society end up in prison because they make bad choices. Some sociologists study why this happens."

## Psychology

### Primitives
- **Operationalization**: Defining abstract concepts as measurable variables
- **Construct validity**: Whether a measure actually captures what it claims to
- **Correlation vs. causation**: The fundamental discipline of distinguishing association from effect
- **Effect size**: Not just statistical significance but practical significance
- **Replication**: Whether findings hold across studies, populations, and contexts
- **Confounds**: Identifying alternative explanations for observed relationships
- **Individual differences**: Resistance to universal claims about "human nature"
- **Ecological validity**: Whether lab findings translate to real-world contexts

### What Strong Writing Looks Like
"Dweck's growth mindset research has been widely popularized, but the effect sizes in replication attempts have been substantially smaller than the original studies. Li and Bates (2019) found no significant effect of mindset intervention on academic performance in their pre-registered replication (n = 624). This doesn't invalidate the concept — but it suggests the popularized version overstates the magnitude."

### What Weak Writing Looks Like
"Research has shown that having a growth mindset leads to success. Studies prove that if you believe you can improve, you will."

## Philosophy

### Primitives
- **Validity**: Whether the conclusion follows from the premises (logical structure)
- **Soundness**: Whether the premises are actually true (truth + validity)
- **The principle of charity**: Interpreting an argument in its strongest possible form before criticizing
- **Thought experiments**: Testing principles against hypothetical scenarios
- **Necessary vs. sufficient conditions**: Precision about what's required vs. what's enough
- **Reductio ad absurdum**: Showing a position leads to absurd conclusions
- **Counterexample**: A single case that disproves a universal claim
- **Distinction-making**: Clarifying what appears to be one concept is actually two or more

### What Strong Writing Looks Like
"Singer's drowning child argument is formally valid: if we ought to prevent suffering when we can do so without comparable moral cost, and if donating to effective charities prevents suffering at minimal personal cost, then we ought to donate. The vulnerable premise is the second — 'minimal personal cost' does significant load-bearing work, and Singer's own lifestyle suggests the threshold is higher than the argument implies."

### What Weak Writing Looks Like
"Peter Singer believes we should help others. His argument makes a lot of sense because it would be wrong to let someone drown if you could easily save them."

## Economics

### Primitives
- **Opportunity cost**: What you give up by choosing one option
- **Marginal analysis**: The cost/benefit of one MORE unit, not the total
- **Incentive structure**: How reward/punishment systems shape behavior
- **Externalities**: Costs or benefits borne by parties outside a transaction
- **Equilibrium**: The state where no party has incentive to change behavior
- **Elasticity**: How responsive quantity is to price changes
- **Moral hazard**: When insulation from risk changes behavior
- **Selection bias**: When the sample systematically differs from the population

### What Strong Writing Looks Like
"Minimum wage debates often focus on employment effects, but the more interesting question is incidence: who actually bears the cost? In competitive labor markets, the textbook answer is split between employer and employee based on relative elasticities. But monopsonistic employers — which describe most low-wage labor markets — can absorb increases with minimal employment effects because they were already paying below the competitive wage."

### What Weak Writing Looks Like
"Raising the minimum wage is a complex issue. Some economists think it helps workers while others worry about job losses. Both sides make valid points."

## Computer Science

### Primitives
- **Abstraction**: Managing complexity by hiding implementation details
- **Decomposition**: Breaking problems into smaller, solvable sub-problems
- **Algorithm analysis**: Evaluating time/space complexity and tradeoffs
- **State management**: Tracking and controlling how data changes over time
- **Concurrency**: Reasoning about operations that happen simultaneously
- **Correctness**: Formal or informal reasoning about whether a system does what it claims
- **Tradeoffs**: Every design decision trades something for something else
- **Edge cases**: Behavior at boundaries, limits, and unexpected inputs

---

## Nursing / Health Sciences

### Primitives
- **Evidence-based practice**: Integrating research evidence, clinical expertise, and patient preference
- **Clinical reasoning**: Pattern recognition → hypothesis generation → targeted assessment → intervention
- **Pathophysiology**: Understanding disease mechanisms, not just symptoms
- **Risk stratification**: Identifying who is most vulnerable and why
- **Therapeutic communication**: Intentional interaction that facilitates healing
- **Systems thinking**: How individual health connects to community, social, and structural factors
- **Ethical reasoning**: Autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice in tension

---

## Usage

When writing in academic mode:

1. Identify the discipline of the assignment
2. Load the relevant primitives
3. Ensure the paper uses AT LEAST 3 disciplinary primitives correctly
4. Flag any passage that uses a primitive incorrectly or superficially
5. Verify that the analysis would be recognizable to a practitioner in the field

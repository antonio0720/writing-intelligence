# Paragraph Shapes

Every paragraph has a shape — an internal architecture that determines how
information flows, tension builds, and the reader exits. Weak paragraphs are
shapeless: they accumulate sentences without progression. Strong paragraphs
have deliberate internal movement.

---

## The Thesis Paragraph

Shape: Claim → Scope → Stakes

Opens with a declarative claim. Second sentence defines the territory (what this
claim covers and what it doesn't). Third sentence establishes why it matters —
the cost of ignoring it or the reward of understanding it.

When to use: Opening a section. Establishing a position. Setting up a debate.

Common failure: The claim is vague. "This is an important issue" is not a thesis.
"Federal water policy subsidizes almond farming at the expense of residential
supply" is.

---

## The Evidence Paragraph

Shape: Claim → Source → Data → Interpretation

Opens with a specific sub-claim. Second sentence names the source. Third gives
the data, quote, or observation. Fourth explains what the evidence means in
context — why it supports the claim and what it doesn't cover.

When to use: Supporting an argument. Building a case. Academic writing.

Common failure: Evidence presented without interpretation. The reader sees the
data but not why it matters. Or: interpretation presented without evidence. The
reader hears the claim but not why they should believe it.

---

## The Contrast Paragraph

Shape: Position A → Position B → Tension → Resolution (or deliberate non-resolution)

Opens with one view. Presents the opposing view. Names where they conflict. Then
either resolves the conflict with a synthesis or leaves the tension standing as
a productive question.

When to use: Comparing approaches. Handling objections. Exploring nuance.

Common failure: The contrast is cosmetic — Position A and Position B are actually
the same thing in different words. Or: the resolution dismisses one side without
engaging its strongest version (strawmanning).

---

## The Narrative Paragraph

Shape: Moment → Detail → Significance

Opens with a specific moment in time. Adds sensory or contextual detail. Closes
by connecting the moment to the larger argument or theme.

When to use: Grounding abstraction. Opening an essay. Illustrating a point.
Breaking up analytical density.

Common failure: The narrative doesn't connect to anything. It's decoration. Every
narrative paragraph must earn its place by advancing the argument, illustrating
the claim, or revealing something that pure analysis can't.

---

## The Escalation Paragraph

Shape: Base claim → Complication → Deeper claim

Opens with an observation. Introduces a complicating factor. Arrives at a harder,
more precise version of the original observation.

When to use: Building intellectual momentum. Moving from obvious to non-obvious.
Deepening analysis.

Common failure: The escalation is fake — the "deeper claim" is just a restatement
of the base claim with bigger words.

---

## The Concession Paragraph

Shape: Opposing point → Genuine acknowledgment → Pivot → Stronger counter

Opens with the strongest version of the opposing argument. Acknowledges what's
true about it. Pivots to the specific point where it fails. Presents the counter
that survives the concession.

When to use: Addressing counterarguments. Building credibility. Academic rebuttals.

Common failure: The concession is insincere — "While some might argue X, this is
clearly wrong because Y." That's not a concession. A real concession costs something.
It admits what's true on the other side and STILL makes its point.

---

## The Scene Paragraph

Shape: Setting → Action → Dialogue or Detail → Implication

Opens with a physical or temporal anchor. Something happens. A specific detail or
line of dialogue crystallizes the moment. The paragraph closes with what the scene
means or lets the reader draw the inference.

When to use: Fiction. Memoir. Narrative nonfiction. Feature journalism. Sermons.

Common failure: Over-interpreting the scene. If the detail is strong enough, let it
land. Not every scene paragraph needs the writer to explain what it means.

---

## The Compression Paragraph

Shape: Dense synthesis in 2-3 sentences

Maximum information density. Every word earns its place. No setup, no exit ramp,
no throat-clearing. Often follows a longer exploratory passage to crystallize
what was just discussed.

When to use: After complexity. As a pivot between major sections. In executive
summaries, strategy documents, and any context where the reader's time is sacred.

Common failure: Confusing compression with vagueness. "Several factors contribute
to this outcome" is short but empty. Compression means SHORT AND FULL, not short.

---

## The Question Paragraph

Shape: Real question → Why it's hard → What's at stake in the answer

Opens with a genuine question (not rhetorical). Explains why the question resists
easy answers. Identifies what hinges on the answer.

When to use: Before exploring an unresolved problem. At section transitions.
When intellectual honesty demands admitting uncertainty.

Common failure: The question is rhetorical. If the writer already knows the answer,
it's not a question paragraph — it's a setup. Setup belongs in a thesis paragraph.

---

## Paragraph Physics Audit Checklist

For every paragraph, verify:

1. Entry force: Does the first sentence compel reading the second?
2. Internal movement: Does the paragraph go somewhere, or does it hover?
3. Sentence variety: At least 2 different sentence lengths within the paragraph?
4. Exit type: Does it launch (→ next paragraph), land (resolve), or hang (tension)?
5. Purpose clarity: Can you name the paragraph's job in one word?
6. Overlap check: Does it repeat the previous paragraph's content?
7. Compression test: Can you cut 20%+ without losing the point?

# Evidence Moves

## Principle

Evidence doesn't speak for itself. Every piece of evidence needs three things:
introduction (what it is and where it comes from), presentation (the evidence
itself), and interpretation (what it means in context). Missing any one of
these three is a failure.

---

## Move Types

### The Data Anchor
Introduce a specific number that grounds the argument.

Pattern: Claim → "In [year], [source] found/reported [specific data]" → Interpretation

Example:
"Remote workers report higher productivity — but the gains aren't distributed
equally. Stanford's 2023 study of 1,600 employees found that fully remote
workers were 10% less productive than hybrid workers, with the gap concentrated
in collaborative tasks. For independent work, the difference vanished."

### The Case Citation
Name a specific instance that illustrates the pattern.

Pattern: General claim → "Consider [specific named case]:" → Details → What it shows

Example:
"Supply chain concentration creates fragile systems. When a single Renesas
semiconductor plant in Naka, Japan caught fire in March 2021, Toyota cut
global production by 500,000 vehicles over three months. One factory. One fire.
Half a million cars."

### The Expert Voice
Bring in a named authority whose expertise is relevant.

Pattern: Context → "[Named expert], [credential], [verb: argues/observes/warns]
that [specific claim]" → Your interpretation

Example:
"The optimism around fusion timelines may be premature. Daniel Jassby, a
retired Princeton plasma physicist with 25 years of tokamak research, has
argued that net-energy-positive fusion remains decades away because published
results routinely exclude the energy cost of operating the magnets and
heating systems."

### The Contrast Pair
Present two pieces of evidence that create tension with each other.

Pattern: Evidence A → Evidence B → "The gap between A and B suggests/reveals..."

Example:
"The World Bank classified 47 countries as 'low income' in 2023. The IMF's
own lending portfolio held active programs in 42 of them. The near-total
overlap raises a question the institutions rarely address publicly: if
multilateral lending were working, would the same countries still qualify
as low-income after three decades of structural adjustment programs?"

### The Temporal Sequence
Show change over time to establish a trend or trajectory.

Pattern: "[Metric] was [X] in [Year A]. By [Year B], it was [Y]." →
Interpretation of the rate, direction, or inflection point.

Example:
"U.S. public trust in newspapers stood at 72% in 1976. By 2023, Gallup
measured it at 16%. The decline wasn't linear — trust held above 50% until
2004, then collapsed. The inflection point coincides not with the rise of
social media but with the industry's shift to digital advertising models
that rewarded engagement over accuracy."

### The Concessive Evidence
Present evidence that works AGAINST your argument, then show why your
argument holds anyway.

Pattern: "The strongest evidence against [my position] is [specific data/case]."
→ Why it doesn't invalidate the argument → What it does reveal

Example:
"The strongest evidence against minimum wage increases is the CBO's 2019
projection that a $15 federal minimum would eliminate 1.3 million jobs. That
number deserves respect. But the same report projected 17 million workers
would see direct income gains and another 10 million would benefit from
spillover effects. The question isn't whether job losses occur — they likely
do at some scale. The question is whether the net effect across 27 million
beneficiaries outweighs losses concentrated in specific sectors."

## Evidence Integration Rules

1. Never drop a quote or statistic without introduction and interpretation
2. Vary signal phrases — don't use "According to" more than twice per 1000 words
3. Paraphrases must change BOTH vocabulary and structure from the original
4. Short quotes (under 15 words) integrate into the sentence flow
5. Long quotes (15+ words) get block formatting with preceding introduction and following analysis
6. Every piece of evidence must connect explicitly to the paragraph's claim
7. If evidence is ambiguous, say so — forced certainty is dishonest
8. Primary sources outrank secondary sources
9. Recent evidence outranks older evidence unless the older evidence is foundational
10. A single strong piece of evidence beats three weak ones

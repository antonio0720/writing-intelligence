# Fake Depth Patterns

These patterns create the appearance of insight without delivering it. They are
the intellectual equivalent of empty calories — satisfying on first bite,
nutritionally void.

---

## The Definitional Loop

"Leadership is about leading. True leaders lead by example, and through their
leadership, they inspire others to lead."

Detection: The paragraph defines a term using the term itself or its cognates.
No new information enters.

Fix: Define through example, contrast, or consequence — not through repetition.

---

## The False Paradox

"The more connected we become, the more disconnected we are."

Detection: Two opposing statements joined without evidence, mechanism, or
qualification. Sounds profound because contrast triggers the brain's
pattern-recognition. But without proof, it's decoration.

Fix: If the paradox is real, prove it. Show the mechanism. Name the conditions
under which it holds. If it's not real, delete it.

---

## The Inflated Stakes

"This decision will determine the future of the entire organization."

Detection: Consequence language that exceeds what the evidence supports. If
every decision "determines the future," none of them do.

Fix: State the actual stakes at their actual scale. "This decision affects Q3
hiring timelines" is weaker-sounding and stronger-writing than fake eschatology.

---

## The Universal Claim

"Everyone knows that..." / "History has shown that..." / "It's human nature to..."

Detection: Universal quantifier without evidence. These are assertions
masquerading as premises.

Fix: Qualify ("many people," "in these three cases," "one tendency") or cite
evidence. The universal claim is almost always false and almost always a sign
the writer hasn't done the work.

---

## The Metaphor Substitution

"Education is the key that unlocks the door to opportunity."

Detection: A metaphor presented as if it constitutes an argument. The metaphor
does not explain mechanism, quantify effect, or address counterarguments. It
just makes the claim sound like it's already been proven.

Fix: Either (a) use the metaphor briefly and follow it with the actual argument,
or (b) drop the metaphor and state the claim directly with evidence.

---

## The Wisdom Posture

"Perhaps the greatest lesson is that there are no easy answers."

Detection: A concluding statement that pretends to offer wisdom while actually
saying nothing. The "lesson" is that there is no lesson.

Fix: If there genuinely are no easy answers, say what makes the question hard.
Name the tensions, tradeoffs, or unresolved evidence. The difficulty IS the content.

---

## The Scope Escape

"While a full exploration of this topic is beyond the scope of this piece..."

Detection: Acknowledging a gap without filling it. In AI output, this is often a
hedge covering the model's inability to go deeper. In human writing, it's sometimes
legitimate — but only if the acknowledged territory is genuinely out of scope.

Fix: Either (a) actually explore the topic if it matters, or (b) cut the sentence
entirely. Pointing at depth you won't provide is worse than silence.

---

## The Authority Borrow

"As Einstein once said..." / "To paraphrase Hemingway..." / "In the words of
Martin Luther King Jr..."

Detection: Invoking a famous name to lend weight to a point the writer could
(and should) make on their own authority.

Fix: Make the point first. If a quote genuinely adds something the writer's own
words cannot, use it. But the quote should support, not replace, the argument.
And verify the quote is real. LLMs fabricate attributions.

---

## The Nuance Performance

"The truth, as always, lies somewhere in the middle."

Detection: Claiming centrism as insight. This is especially common in LLM output
because models are trained toward balanced, non-committal positions.

Fix: If the middle position is defensible, defend it with specific evidence and
explain exactly where in the middle. If one side is actually stronger, say so.
Equidistance is not wisdom — it's avoidance.

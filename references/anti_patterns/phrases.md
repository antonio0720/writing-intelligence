## 1. Hard Bans

These phrases appear almost exclusively in LLM output. Remove on sight. No exceptions.

### Setup / Throat-Clearing
- "Here's the thing"
- "Here's the deal"
- "Here's what you need to know"
- "Let's be clear"
- "Let's be honest"
- "Let's break it down"
- "Let's unpack this"
- "Let's dive in"
- "Let's explore"
- "Make no mistake"
- "To be sure"
- "It's worth noting"
- "It goes without saying"
- "It bears mentioning"
- "It's important to understand"
- "It's crucial to recognize"
- "It's no secret that"

### Temporal Cliché
- "In today's world"
- "In today's landscape"
- "In today's economy"
- "In an era of"
- "In a world where"
- "In the ever-evolving"
- "In this day and age"
- "As we navigate"
- "As we stand at the crossroads"

### Pseudo-Depth
- "At its core"
- "At the end of the day"
- "When all is said and done"
- "When the dust settles"
- "The real question is"
- "The question isn't X — it's Y"
- "It's not just X — it's Y"
- "Not only X, but Y"
- "It's less about X and more about Y"

### Manufactured Drama
- "Think about it"
- "Let that sink in"
- "Read that again"
- "Spoiler alert"
- "Plot twist"
- "Here's the kicker"
- "Wait for it"
- "Brace yourself"

### Punctuation as Mic
- "Full stop."
- "Period."
- "Mic drop."
- "End of story."
- "That's it. That's the post."

### Buzzword Compounds
- "Game-changer"
- "Paradigm shift"
- "Double-edged sword"
- "Silver bullet"
- "North star"
- "Low-hanging fruit"
- "Move the needle"
- "Deep dive"
- "Unpack"
- "Lean in"
- "Level up"
- "Circle back"

### False Intimacy
- "I see you"
- "I hear you"
- "You're not alone in this"
- "And that's okay"
- "Give yourself grace"
- "Your feelings are valid"
- "It's okay to not be okay"
(Allowed ONLY in therapeutic, pastoral, or explicitly personal contexts with a real referent)

## 2. Soft Bans

Remove unless they demonstrably earn their place. The test: does removing it change the meaning or force of the sentence? If not, it goes.

### Adverbs
- Remove ornamental adverbs: "truly," "really," "very," "incredibly," "extremely," "absolutely," "fundamentally," "essentially," "literally" (figurative), "undeniably," "unquestionably," "remarkably"
- Keep precision adverbs ONLY when removal changes factual meaning: "She arrived late" (keep), "She arrived incredibly" (cut)
- Keep manner adverbs when they specify how: "He spoke quietly" is fine. "He spoke really quietly" loses "really."

### Em Dashes
- Default: remove. Replace with period, comma, colon, or restructure.
- Exception: retain when the em dash creates a timing effect that no other punctuation achieves. One per 500 words maximum.

### Semicolons
- Allowed in formal/academic prose and lists
- Banned in casual, conversational, or sales prose
- Never use to connect unrelated clauses

### "However" as Opener
- Default: restructure the contrast into the sentence
- Exception: allowed when the contrast is the paragraph's entire point and "however" opens cleanly

### Parenthetical Asides
- Under 8 words: allowed
- Over 8 words: restructure as a separate sentence or footnote

### "And" / "But" as Sentence Openers
- Maximum: once per 500 words each
- Must serve rhythm, not laziness

## 3. Earned Exceptions

These moves are powerful when intentional and weak when habitual. Allow only with clear purpose.

### Rhetorical Questions
- **Allowed**: When they open genuine tension or redirect the reader's frame
- **Banned**: When they substitute for argument ("Isn't that what we all want?")
- Test: Does the question create uncertainty? Or does it assume its own answer?

### One-Sentence Paragraphs
- **Allowed**: For impact after a dense passage, or to mark a pivot
- **Banned**: More than once per 800 words. Never consecutive.
- Test: Would combining it with the adjacent paragraph lose force?

### Fragment Sentences
- **Allowed**: For rhythmic punch in narrative, speech, or sales
- **Banned**: In academic, legal, or precision prose
- Test: Is the fragment intentional craft or incomplete thought?

### Intentional Repetition
- **Allowed**: Anaphora, epistrophe, or structural repetition for rhetorical force
- **Banned**: Same idea restated in different words (padding)
- Test: Remove the repetition. Did you lose rhythm or just words?

### Manufactured Quotables
- **Banned**: Lines formatted for pull-quote extraction but hollow in context
- **Allowed**: Lines that earn memorability through the quality of the thought
- Test: Is this memorable because of what it says, or how it's punctuated?

## 4. Inflated Verbs

Replace with precise, honest alternatives:

| Inflated | Usually Means |
|---|---|
| revolutionize | change, improve, redesign |
| transform | change, shift, adjust |
| reimagine | redesign, rethink |
| redefine | change the definition of, expand |
| disrupt | compete with, challenge, enter |
| empower | enable, allow, give authority to |
| leverage | use |
| optimize | improve, tune, adjust |
| synergize | combine, coordinate |
| catalyze | start, accelerate |
| democratize | make accessible |
| spearhead | lead |
| champion | support, advocate |
| unlock | enable, reveal |
| amplify | increase, strengthen |
| elevate | improve, raise |

Use the inflated verb ONLY when the scale of the action genuinely warrants it.
"The printing press revolutionized information access" = earned.
"Our new dashboard revolutionizes analytics" = inflated.

## 5. Filler Hedges

Remove unless they reflect genuine epistemic uncertainty:

- "It's important to note that" → just state the note
- "It should be mentioned that" → mention it
- "One could argue that" → make the argument or attribute it
- "There is a sense in which" → specify the sense
- "In many ways" → name the ways or cut it
- "To some extent" → specify the extent or cut it
- "Broadly speaking" → be specific or cut it
- "By and large" → specify or cut
- "For all intents and purposes" → cut
- "At the risk of oversimplifying" → just simplify honestly

## 6. AI Signature Transitions

These transition patterns are statistically overrepresented in LLM output. Vary or replace:

- "Moreover" (once per 1000 words max)
- "Furthermore" (once per 1000 words max)
- "Additionally" (once per 1500 words max)
- "That said" (once per 1000 words max)
- "With that in mind" (once per 2000 words max)
- "This is where" (once per 1500 words max)
- "This brings us to" (hard ban)
- "Which brings us to" (hard ban)
- "Building on this" (hard ban)
- "Taken together" (once per 2000 words max)

Preferred alternatives: structural contrast, juxtaposition, temporal markers,
causal connectors, or simply starting the new paragraph with its own claim.
The strongest transition is no transition — just a paragraph that earns its
adjacency to the previous one.

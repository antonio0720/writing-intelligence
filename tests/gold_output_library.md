# Gold Output Library

## Purpose

as target examples when the compiler is generating or evaluating output.

## Academic Essay (Score: 92)

Voice: academic_rigorous | Genre: academic | Audience: professor

"The standard explanation for declining union membership — that globalization
and automation made collective bargaining obsolete — treats the outcome as
inevitable. It wasn't. Canada experienced the same economic pressures between
1970 and 2020 and maintained union density above 30%, while the U.S. figure
dropped from 27% to 10.3% (Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2023). The divergence
points to institutional design, not economic determinism.

Specifically, three legislative differences explain most of the gap. Card-check
certification, which Canada retained and the U.S. effectively eliminated after
Taft-Hartley, reduced the employer's ability to delay and discourage organizing
campaigns. First-contract arbitration, available in most Canadian provinces,
prevented the pattern — documented by Bronfenbrenner (2009) in 42% of
successful U.S. certification elections — where employers simply refused to
negotiate a first contract until the union dissolved. And sectoral bargaining
provisions in Quebec and British Columbia established wage floors that
individual-firm bargaining in the U.S. could not sustain against competitive
pressure.

None of this means the Canadian model is without problems. Doorey (2017)
has shown that even with stronger legal protections, Canadian organizing rates
in the private sector have declined since 2000, suggesting that legal
infrastructure is necessary but not sufficient. The question is whether
institutional design creates the conditions for collective action or merely
fails to prevent its erosion."

Why this scores 92:
- Named sources with dates
- Specific data (30%, 10.3%, 42%)
- Counterargument engaged honestly
- Three-mechanism explanation (not vague)
- Conclusion extends beyond the evidence
- Sentence lengths: 21, 3, 29, 27, 38, 31, 25, 16, 33

## Strategy Memo (Score: 94)

Voice: sovereign_commander | Genre: strategy | Audience: executive

"Revenue is $8.4M ARR. It has been flat for three quarters. Net revenue
retention dropped from 108% to 94% since Q1. Two top-ten accounts are in
active procurement review with competitors.

The cause is product stagnation. Our platform serves the mid-market buyer
who signed in 2022. That buyer now has 14 alternatives that didn't exist at
signing. We haven't shipped a differentiation feature since the dashboard
redesign in September 2024.

Recommendation: Ship the API integration layer by June 30. Seven of our ten
at-risk accounts have requested this specific feature. Engineering estimates
11 weeks. I'm requesting 8 by pulling two engineers from the mobile project,
which can absorb a 6-week delay without revenue impact.

Risk: If we miss June 30, the two accounts in procurement review will likely
churn — $1.2M ARR. If we pull the mobile engineers, the Q4 mobile launch
slips to Q1 2027. The cost of inaction exceeds the cost of delay.

Decision required by Friday: approve the engineering reallocation. I brief
the mobile team Monday."

Why this scores 94:
- Every claim has a number
- Owner assigned to every action
- Risk quantified in both directions
- Decision point is specific with deadline
- Zero buzzwords
- Sentence lengths: 5, 8, 12, 15, 18, 21, 8, 29, 19, 16, 11

## Fiction (Score: 93)

Voice: literary_recursive | Genre: fiction | Audience: literary reader

"The keys went on the counter. Not in the bowl — the ceramic one her mother
had brought back from Oaxaca, the one that had held every set of keys in this
house for eleven years. On the counter. Next to the mail she hadn't opened.

Marcus watched her from the doorway. He had a specific stillness for moments
like this, a quality of attention she used to find comforting and now recognized
as assessment. He was calculating distance. Not physical distance — the other
kind. The kind measured in how many truths you could survive telling at once.

'I called the restaurant,' she said.

He didn't move.

'She thought I was funny.' Elena opened the refrigerator, studied its contents
as if they might resolve something. Closed it. 'Marta. She actually laughed
when I said you'd been working late there. Because you haven't been. Not in
weeks.'

The clock above the stove said 11:40. The house had that specific silence
that happens after the dishwasher finishes — a silence with a shape, aware
of itself.

'I want you to pack a bag tonight,' she said. The words were level. Not calm
— level. The way a surgeon's voice is level. Precision, not peace. 'We can
figure out the rest when I've decided if there's a rest to figure out.'"

Why this scores 93:
- Emotion shown through objects and actions (keys on counter, not in bowl)
- Specific sensory details (ceramic bowl from Oaxaca, clock time, dishwasher silence)
- Dialogue carries subtext (what's NOT said matters more)
- No dialogue adverbs. No emotion-naming.
- Sentence lengths: 6, 32, 3, 8, 22, 30, 5, 3, 28, 6, 11, 32, 24, 14, 7, 23

## Sermon (Score: 91)

Voice: sermon_black_church | Genre: sermon | Audience: congregation

"I want to tell you about a Wednesday. Not a special Wednesday — not a
holiday, not a birthday, not a day anybody would remember except the woman
it happened to. A Wednesday in October, three years ago. She was sitting in
the parking lot of the H-E-B on Riverside. Engine off. Groceries in the back
seat. And she couldn't move.

Not because anything was wrong with the car. Not because she was hurt. Because
for the first time in her life, she was asking herself a question she didn't
have the answer to: 'What am I doing this for?'

Some of you know that parking lot. Some of you know that question.

The Psalmist writes in Psalm 40: 'I waited patiently for the Lord; and He
inclined unto me, and heard my cry. He brought me up also out of a horrible
pit, out of the miry clay, and set my feet upon a rock.'

But here's what I need you to hear today. The Psalmist didn't say God reached
down while he was walking. He said God reached down while he was stuck. In the
pit. In the clay. Not moving. Not climbing. Stuck.

God doesn't need you to be in motion to find you. God doesn't need you to
have it figured out. God doesn't need you standing. God meets you sitting in
the parking lot with the engine off and the groceries going warm.

That woman drove home that night. She unpacked the groceries. She put the kids
to bed. And the next Sunday — this is the part — the next Sunday she walked
into this building. And she's here today. Some of you know who I'm talking
about. Some of you ARE who I'm talking about."

Why this scores 91:
- Specific testimony (parking lot, H-E-B on Riverside, October, Wednesday)
- Scripture integrated, not block-quoted — and then INTERPRETED
- Cadence builds: conversational → scriptural → prophetic → intimate
- Anaphora ("God doesn't need you...") at climax
- Congregation directly addressed and respected
- Sentence lengths vary dramatically: 7, 38, 7, 8, 5, 24, 14, 6, 22, 9, 31, 15, 6, 7, 8, 13, 25, 14

## Sales Copy (Score: 90)

Voice: casual_sharp | Genre: sales | Audience: SaaS buyer

"Your sales team spends 11 hours a week on data entry. Not selling. Entering
data. That's 572 hours per rep per year — the equivalent of 71 full working
days — spent typing information into a CRM that nobody trusts anyway.

We measured this across 340 B2B sales teams. The number wasn't 11 hours
because we did something clever with the math. It was 11 hours because we
watched. Screen recordings. Time stamps. The actual minutes between 'close
the deal' and 'the deal shows up in Salesforce correctly.'

Drift CRM eliminates the data entry. Not reduces. Eliminates. Every call is
transcribed, every email is parsed, every deal stage is updated automatically
based on what actually happened — not what someone remembered to log at 6 PM
on a Friday.

The result for the 340 teams in our pilot: reps recovered an average of 9.2
hours per week. Pipeline accuracy improved from 61% to 94%. And revenue per
rep increased 23% — not because the product made them better sellers, but
because they were actually selling instead of typing.

14-day trial. Full data migration. If your pipeline accuracy doesn't improve
by at least 15 points in the first month, we refund the setup fee. No
questions, no exit interview, no 'let us try one more thing' email."

Why this scores 90:
- Specific data (11 hours, 572 hours, 340 teams, 61% to 94%)
- Proof before promise (measurement methodology explained)
- Guarantee is specific and verifiable (15 points)
- Voice is conversational but credible
- No manufactured urgency
- Risk reversal is clear and generous

## Government Brief (Score: 89)

Voice: investor_precision (adapted) | Genre: government_brief | Audience: agency director

"SUBJECT: Decision Required — FY2026 Water Infrastructure Grant Allocation

ISSUE: The Department must allocate $47.3M in remaining FY2026 DWSRF funds
by September 30 or forfeit the balance under 42 U.S.C. § 300j-12(a)(4).

BACKGROUND: 14 eligible applications remain in review. Three exceed $10M and
require Assistant Secretary concurrence per Departmental Policy 430.1. Current
staff capacity can process approximately 8 applications before the deadline
at standard review pace.

OPTIONS:

Option A — Prioritize by population served. Process the 8 applications
covering the largest populations first. Allocates $38.1M. Remaining $9.2M
and 6 applications forfeit or carry to FY2027 under the extension provision.
Risk: Two small-system applications with acute contamination issues
(Denton County, TX; Lowndes County, AL) would not be processed.

Option B — Prioritize by health risk severity. Process the 3 acute
contamination cases first, then fill remaining capacity by population.
Allocates $41.6M across 10 applications. Risk: Processing timeline is
tighter. Requires overtime authorization for the review team.

RECOMMENDATION: Option B. The $3.5M additional allocation and coverage of
acute contamination cases outweigh the overtime cost (estimated $34,000).
The two contamination cases involve documented health impacts currently
under EPA enforcement action.

COORDINATION: OCSPP concurs. OW concurs. Budget Office has confirmed
overtime authority exists under current CR.

DECISION NEEDED BY: August 15, 2026."

Why this scores 89:
- BLUF structure (issue stated in first sentence)
- Statutory citation for the deadline
- Options presented with specific dollar amounts and risks
- Recommendation is specific with cost-benefit reasoning
- Coordination documented
- Decision deadline stated
